14  Naming Ourselves and Others

A group begins to exist publicly when it can be named. Before naming, people may share experience, location, condition, interest, injury, work, ancestry, belief, or desire, but without a name, the shared condition may remain scattered. Individuals may feel something separately without recognizing that others are living inside the same structure. Naming gathers them: it turns scattered people into a recognizable group, private experience into shared identity, difference into distinction, and many individuals into a possible “we.” To name a group is not merely to attach a label but to create a social object that can be recognized, remembered, addressed, organized, protected, criticized, excluded, or mobilized. This chapter explains why naming is one of the most important acts of rhetorical language.

14.1 Naming as Recognition and Collective Subject

Naming creates recognition. When people can say “we are workers,” “we are students,” “we are citizens,” “we are builders,” or “we are survivors,” they are not merely describing themselves but making themselves recognizable as a collective subject. Recognition means that a group can appear in language: others can refer to it, members can identify with it, institutions can classify it, opponents can challenge it, allies can support it, and the group can speak in its own name. Without a name, a group may exist socially but remain weak rhetorically because it cannot easily demand attention, claim responsibility, defend boundaries, preserve memory, or coordinate action. A name gives the group a handle on itself: it allows people to say who they are, what happened to them, what they stand for, what they refuse, and what they ask the world to recognize.

Naming is therefore the first architecture of group identity. It gathers scattered experience into a shared object that can be publicly recognized. Before naming, people may only feel confusion, discomfort, or isolated pain; after naming, they can say “this is what is happening,” and the phenomenon becomes available to collective thought. This is one of the deepest powers of public language: it converts dispersed experience into recognizable reality. A workplace problem becomes “burnout,” a social pattern becomes “discrimination,” a market shift becomes “platform dependency.” The name does not create the entire phenomenon, but it creates public access to it and makes it discussable.

Summary of this section. Naming creates recognition by turning scattered individuals into a collective subject that can appear in language. Without a name, a group cannot easily coordinate or demand attention. Naming converts private experience into shared reality and is the first architecture of group identity.

14.2 Self-Naming and Other-Naming

There is a major difference between naming ourselves and being named by others. Self-naming is an act of agency: a group chooses the language through which it understands itself and says “this is the name under which we recognize our shared life.” Other-naming is an act of classification from outside: another person, group, institution, or authority assigns a name and says “this is what you are.” Self-naming can create dignity, solidarity, and collective voice; other-naming can create recognition but also domination, distortion, and stigma. A group named from outside may be reduced to what others see, fear, need, or control. It may be named by its labor, its danger, its difference, its usefulness, or its supposed defect.

A group that names itself can resist reduction by saying that the outside name is not their name, that the category does not fit, or that the label hides what they actually are. Many struggles over identity are therefore struggles over naming: who has the authority to name, who must live under the name, what history the name carries, what power it serves, and what future it makes possible. Naming is never neutral when it affects belonging, status, dignity, or rights.

The pronoun “we” is one of the most powerful words in collective language. It can include, invite, claim, command, pressure, or erase. “We need to act” creates shared responsibility; “we have suffered” creates shared memory; “we do not accept this” creates a boundary. But the force of “we” depends on who is included and who is authorized to speak. When “we” is honest, it creates collective agency; when it is manipulative, it pressures individuals into agreement; when it is exclusionary, it turns outsiders into threats. A speaker who uses “we” must be accountable to the group being invoked by asking who is included, who has consented, who bears the cost, and who benefits from presenting the group as unified.

Summary of this section. Self-naming gives a group agency over its identity; other-naming may recognize but also distort or dominate. Struggles over identity are often struggles over naming. The pronoun “we” is powerful but must be accountable to the group it invokes, as it can create solidarity or manufacture false unity.

14.3 Names Create Group Boundaries

A name creates a boundary. To say “we” is to mark membership and distinguish those included from those outside. This boundary may be formal or informal, open or closed, flexible or rigid. A professional title distinguishes trained members from outsiders; a family name distinguishes kinship; a national name distinguishes citizenship or cultural belonging; a movement name distinguishes participants from nonparticipants. Boundaries are necessary because groups cannot coordinate without knowing who belongs, who speaks, who decides, who is responsible, and who is affected. But boundaries become dangerous when they harden into moral absolutes: the boundary between “we” and “they” can clarify responsibility but also create superiority; it can organize solidarity but also organize hostility.

The distinction between “us” and “them” is one of the oldest structures of group language. It is not always wrong: a team must know who is on the team, a community must know whom it serves, a movement must know what it opposes. But “us and them” becomes dangerous when it hardens into absolute moral division that says we are pure and they are corrupt, we are rational and they are irrational, we are human and they are less human. Such language reduces complexity, prepares conflict, and allows a group to stop listening or recognizing shared humanity. Responsible boundary language preserves moral judgment without totalization: it can say that an action is wrong or an institution is unjust without saying that those on the other side are nothing but evil.

Internal difference also matters. No group is completely uniform; every group contains difference in age, gender, class, experience, belief, and power. A group name can hide these differences by implying that all members share the same experience and erasing minority voices within the group. Responsible identity language must hold unity and difference together, allowing “we” without pretending that “we” has no internal structure. A mature group can share identity while acknowledging that not all members experience it the same way.

Summary of this section. Names create boundaries that are necessary for coordination but dangerous when they harden into moral absolutes that dehumanize outsiders. Responsible boundary language distinguishes without degrading. Internal difference within groups must be acknowledged so that “we” does not erase minority voices.

14.4 Naming, Dignity, and Stigma

Names can give dignity. A group that has been treated as invisible, inferior, shameful, or abnormal can use a new name to create a different public relation to itself, moving from being an object of others’ judgment to being a subject of its own speech. Dignifying names replace insult with self-description, shame with recognition, isolation with shared identity, and imposed category with chosen meaning. To be named with dignity is to be recognized as a bearer of meaning rather than as an object of classification. But dignity does not come from positive wording alone; a name gains dignity when it connects to real recognition, rights, voice, and respect.

Names can also create stigma. A stigmatizing name marks a group as inferior, dangerous, dirty, backward, weak, irrational, criminal, or shameful. Such names do not merely insult but organize social perception: once a group is stigmatized, its members may be interpreted through suspicion before they speak, their actions judged more harshly, their pain dismissed, their success treated as exception, their anger treated as threat. Stigma turns a name into a social wound that changes how others see the group and how the group may come to see itself. Resisting stigma often begins with language: the group must challenge the name, expose the frame, and replace the imposed meaning with a more truthful account.

Institutions classify groups through names that may be legal, administrative, educational, medical, or demographic. Classification can make a group visible and allow it to receive protection, resources, representation, or legal standing. Without official naming, harm may remain unmeasured and unaddressed. But classification can also reduce people to what the institution needs to process: applicant, patient, beneficiary, offender, case, risk group. These names are useful for administration but incomplete as human descriptions. Institutional naming becomes dangerous when the administrative category replaces the person and people become manageable through the name without being understood.

Summary of this section. Names can give dignity or create stigma. Dignifying names allow groups to speak as subjects rather than objects of classification. Stigmatizing names organize social perception and become social wounds. Institutional classification can make groups visible but also reduce them to administrative categories.

14.5 Naming and Power

Power often works through naming. Those with power can define categories, assign labels, write official records, control public vocabulary, and decide which names become legitimate. They can name themselves as protectors, reformers, guardians, or experts while naming others as threats, burdens, failures, or problems. Such naming can shape the entire field of judgment: if the powerful can name the situation, they can often control what questions are asked; if they can name the affected people, they can influence whether those people are heard; if they can name the conflict, they can shape what solutions seem reasonable.

Counter-power often begins by renaming. It says “this is not security but control,” “this is not tradition but exclusion,” “this is not efficiency but exploitation,” “this is not neutrality but silence.” Renaming contests the dominant frame. This is why struggles over words are rarely just semantics: words decide what kind of reality becomes publicly available. To name a group or situation is to shape the social world, and controlling the language of naming is therefore a fundamental form of power.

Names carry historical memory. A name may preserve migration, struggle, work, region, ancestry, faith, profession, resistance, trauma, achievement, or founding promise. To say the name is often to invoke the story, which is why names can become sacred or contested. They may carry suffering that outsiders do not understand or pride that members wish to protect. The question is not simply whether a name is old or new but what memory it preserves and what relation to reality it creates now. A responsible group must ask what history its name carries, who was included and excluded, whether the name still fits present reality, and whether it preserves dignity or harm.

Summary of this section. Power operates through naming by controlling public vocabulary and defining categories. Counter-power begins by renaming and contesting dominant frames. Names carry historical memory and can become sacred or contested; responsible naming remains answerable to present reality.

14.6 Renaming and Ethical Responsibility

Renaming is one of the strongest acts of identity transformation. To rename is to declare that the old language no longer fits. A group may rename itself to reject stigma, mark a new political consciousness, recover historical memory, clarify mission, escape imposed categories, or signal a change in values. Renaming says that the group is not what it was called, will no longer live under that frame, and is choosing a different public meaning. Renaming can be liberating when it gives a group new dignity, energy, and direction. But renaming can also be superficial: a group may change its name without changing its structure, or an institution may adopt new language while preserving old power. Renaming must be tested by whether it changes recognition, behavior, rights, responsibility, and relation rather than merely improving appearance.

A name can assign responsibility. When a group calls itself “guardians,” “professionals,” “citizens,” “teachers,” or “leaders,” the name carries expectations and obligates. To accept the name is to accept a standard: a teacher must teach, a doctor must care, a leader must carry consequence. Identity names become empty when separated from responsibility. The ethical test of a group name is whether the group lives the responsibility the name implies.

Because naming creates identity, boundary, and social reality, it carries ethical responsibility. A responsible name should clarify rather than distort, recognize dignity rather than produce stigma, create boundary without dehumanization, preserve memory without trapping the group in the past, support unity without erasing internal difference, assign responsibility without creating false guilt, and remain open to revision when reality changes. An irresponsible name may reduce, insult, manipulate, erase, exaggerate, or control. The key ethical questions are who is naming whom, whether the named group accepts the name, what history it carries, what boundary it creates, what emotion it activates, what action it prepares, and what reality it makes visible or invisible. Naming is not a minor act but the beginning of social architecture.

Summary of this section. Renaming declares that old language no longer fits and can liberate identity, but it must be tested by whether it changes reality or only appearance. Names carry responsibility for the standards they imply. Ethical naming clarifies, dignifies, distinguishes appropriately, and remains open to revision.

14.7 Chapter Summary

This chapter explains how language creates group identity, distinction, and social boundary. A group begins to exist publicly when it can be named. Self-naming gives agency over identity, while other-naming may recognize or dominate. Naming creates boundaries that distinguish “we” from “they,” which are necessary but dangerous when they harden into dehumanization. Names can give dignity or create stigma, and they carry historical memory and power.

14.7.1 Essential Knowledge

The following points are the core of this chapter and must be understood and retained.

First, naming is the first architecture of group identity. A group begins to exist publicly when it can be named. Naming turns scattered individuals into a recognizable collective subject that can be addressed, defended, and mobilized. To name is to create a social object.

Second, self-naming is an act of agency; other-naming can be an act of domination. Struggles over identity are often struggles over naming: who has the authority to name, what history the name carries, and whether the named group accepts the name imposed on it.

Third, names create boundaries. The distinction between “we” and “they” is necessary for coordination but dangerous when it hardens into absolute moral division that dehumanizes outsiders. Responsible naming distinguishes without degrading.

Fourth, names carry dignity or stigma. A stigmatizing name marks a group as inferior and organizes social perception against it; a dignifying name allows a group to speak as a subject rather than an object of classification. Resisting stigma often begins with language.

Fifth, names carry responsibility. To accept a name such as teacher, guardian, or professional is to accept a standard. The ethical test of a group name is whether the group lives the responsibility the name implies. Naming is not a minor act but the beginning of social architecture.
