14  Naming Ourselves and Others

A group begins to exist publicly when it can be named.

Before naming, people may share experience, location, condition, interest, injury, work, ancestry, belief, or desire. But without a name, the shared condition may remain scattered. Individuals may feel something separately without recognizing that others are living inside the same structure.

Naming gathers them.

It turns scattered people into a recognizable group.
It turns private experience into shared identity.
It turns difference into distinction.
It turns social position into public form.
It turns “many individuals” into a possible “we.”

To name a group is not merely to attach a label. It is to create a social object that can be recognized, remembered, addressed, organized, protected, criticized, excluded, or mobilized.

This is why naming is one of the most important acts of rhetorical language.

14.1 Naming as Recognition

Naming creates recognition.

When people can say “we are workers,” “we are students,” “we are citizens,” “we are immigrants,” “we are builders,” “we are survivors,” “we are researchers,” “we are parents,” or “we are a community,” they are not merely describing themselves. They are making themselves recognizable as a collective subject.

Recognition means that a group can appear in language.

Others can refer to it.
Members can identify with it.
Institutions can classify it.
Opponents can challenge it.
Allies can support it.
The group can speak in its own name.

Without a name, a group may exist socially but remain weak rhetorically. It cannot easily demand attention, claim responsibility, defend boundaries, preserve memory, or coordinate action.

A name gives the group a handle on itself.

It allows people to say:

This is who we are.
This is what happened to us.
This is what we stand for.
This is what we refuse.
This is what others misunderstand about us.
This is what we ask the world to recognize.

Naming is therefore the first architecture of group identity.

14.2 Self-Naming and Other-Naming

There is a major difference between naming ourselves and being named by others.

Self-naming is an act of agency. A group chooses or claims the language through which it understands itself. It says, “This is the name under which we recognize our shared life.”

Other-naming is an act of classification from outside. Another person, group, institution, authority, or public assigns a name. It says, “This is what you are.”

Both forms are powerful.

Self-naming can create dignity, solidarity, and collective voice.
Other-naming can create recognition, but also domination, distortion, and stigma.

A group named from outside may be reduced to what others see, fear, need, or control. It may be named by its labor, its danger, its difference, its usefulness, its supposed defect, or its relationship to dominant power.

A group that names itself can resist this reduction.

It can say:

That is not our name.
That is not the meaning of our experience.
That category does not fit us.
That label hides what we actually are.
We will speak in our own terms.

Many struggles over identity are therefore struggles over naming.

Who has the authority to name?
Who must live under the name?
What history does the name carry?
What power does the name serve?
What future does the name make possible?

Naming is never neutral when it affects belonging, status, dignity, or rights.

14.3 Names Create Group Boundaries

A name creates a boundary.

To say “we” is to mark some form of membership. It distinguishes those included in the name from those outside it. This boundary may be formal or informal, open or closed, flexible or rigid, gentle or hostile.

A professional title distinguishes trained members from outsiders.
A family name distinguishes kinship.
A national name distinguishes citizens or cultural belonging.
A movement name distinguishes participants from nonparticipants.
A religious name distinguishes shared belief and practice.
A company name distinguishes organizational membership.
A class name distinguishes social position.
A generation name distinguishes historical experience.

Boundaries are necessary because groups cannot coordinate without knowing who belongs, who speaks, who decides, who is responsible, and who is affected.

But boundaries are dangerous when they become moral absolutes.

The boundary between “we” and “they” can clarify responsibility.
It can also create superiority.
It can organize solidarity.
It can also organize hostility.
It can protect a group from erasure.
It can also erase the humanity of others.

A mature group identity requires boundary discipline. It must know where it begins and ends without turning difference into dehumanization.

14.4 The Pronoun “We”

The word “we” is one of the most powerful words in collective language.

It can include, invite, claim, command, pressure, or erase.

“We need to act” creates shared responsibility.
“We have suffered” creates shared memory.
“We believe” creates shared conviction.
“We do not accept this” creates a boundary.
“We are better than this” creates a moral standard.
“We all know” can create false consensus.
“We decided” can hide who actually decided.
“We must sacrifice” can distribute cost unevenly.

The force of “we” depends on who is included and who is authorized to speak.

When “we” is honest, it creates collective agency.
When “we” is careless, it erases internal difference.
When “we” is manipulative, it pressures individuals into agreement.
When “we” is exclusionary, it turns outsiders into threats.

A speaker who uses “we” must be accountable to the group being invoked.

Who is this “we”?
Who is included?
Who is excluded?
Who has consented to this voice?
Who bears the cost of the action proposed in the name of “we”?
Who benefits from presenting the group as unified?

The pronoun “we” can create solidarity, but it can also manufacture unity where real disagreement exists.

14.5 Naming and Dignity

Names can give dignity.

A group may have been treated as invisible, inferior, shameful, or abnormal. A new name can create a different public relation to itself. It can move from being an object of others’ judgment to being a subject of its own speech.

Dignifying names often do several things.

They replace insult with self-description.
They replace shame with recognition.
They replace isolation with shared identity.
They replace imposed category with chosen meaning.
They create a language in which members can stand upright.

For example, a group may reject a name that frames it as deficient and choose a name that frames it through agency, history, culture, work, or shared struggle. The new name is not cosmetic. It reorganizes the moral field.

To be named with dignity is to be recognized as a bearer of meaning, not merely as an object of classification.

But dignity does not come from positive wording alone. A name gains dignity when it connects to real recognition, real rights, real voice, and real respect. A polite label without changed relation becomes symbolic management.

Language can open the door to dignity. It cannot replace the practices that confirm it.

14.6 Naming and Stigma

Names can also create stigma.

A stigmatizing name marks a group as inferior, dangerous, dirty, backward, weak, irrational, criminal, abnormal, or shameful. Such names do not merely insult. They organize social perception.

Once a group is stigmatized, its members may be interpreted through suspicion before they speak. Their actions may be judged more harshly. Their pain may be dismissed. Their success may be treated as exception. Their anger may be treated as threat. Their needs may be treated as burden.

Stigma turns a name into a social wound.

It changes how others see the group and how the group may come to see itself. Repeated stigmatizing language can become internalized. People may begin to carry the outside name inside their own self-understanding.

This is why resisting stigma often begins with language.

A group must challenge the name, expose the frame, and replace the imposed meaning with a more truthful account.

Stigma says:

You are less than the norm.

Counter-naming says:

The norm itself must be examined.

14.7 Naming and Classification

Institutions classify groups through names.

These classifications may be legal, administrative, educational, medical, economic, professional, demographic, or political. They appear in forms, records, policies, statistics, rights, obligations, eligibility rules, and official documents.

Classification can make a group visible.

A recognized category may allow people to receive protection, resources, representation, data tracking, or legal standing. Without official naming, harm may remain unmeasured and unaddressed.

But classification can also reduce.

An institution may name people according to what it needs to process: applicant, patient, beneficiary, offender, taxpayer, employee, case, risk group, customer segment, dependent, resident, user.

These names are useful for administration, but incomplete as human descriptions.

Institutional naming becomes dangerous when the administrative category replaces the person or group. People become manageable through the name, but not necessarily understood.

Responsible classification must remain open to challenge.

Does this category fit?
Who created it?
What does it make visible?
What does it hide?
What procedure does it activate?
What consequence follows from being placed inside it?
Can people speak back to the category?

A category is not merely a label. It is a pathway through a system.

14.8 Naming and Difference

Naming creates distinction.

Groups need distinction because identity requires shape. A group that cannot distinguish itself from anything else cannot know what it is. Difference allows a group to say what is specific about its experience, role, value, memory, or responsibility.

But distinction can take different forms.

It can be descriptive: “We have this history.”
It can be functional: “We perform this role.”
It can be ethical: “We commit to this standard.”
It can be cultural: “We preserve this practice.”
It can be political: “We organize around this demand.”
It can be defensive: “We protect ourselves from this harm.”
It can be hierarchical: “We are above them.”
It can be hostile: “They are the enemy.”

The ethical quality of naming depends partly on how difference is used.

Difference can support clarity, dignity, and responsibility. It can help groups understand one another without collapsing into sameness. But difference can also become hierarchy, contempt, exclusion, or domination.

Responsible naming distinguishes without degrading.

It allows groups to be different without turning difference into a license for dehumanization.

14.9 Naming the Other

Every group names others.

It names allies, strangers, opponents, authorities, outsiders, enemies, guests, competitors, beneficiaries, threats, and partners. These names shape how the group behaves toward those outside itself.

The name “neighbor” invites one kind of relation.
The name “competitor” invites another.
The name “enemy” invites another.
The name “guest” invites another.
The name “invader” invites another.
The name “customer” invites another.
The name “burden” invites another.
The name “stakeholder” invites another.

To name another is to frame the relationship.

This is ethically serious because the name often precedes action. If a group names others as threats, it becomes easier to justify control. If it names them as inferior, it becomes easier to justify exclusion. If it names them as partners, cooperation becomes imaginable. If it names them as neighbors, obligation becomes more personal.

The language of other-naming often reveals the moral structure of a group.

How a group names outsiders shows what kind of group it is becoming.

14.10 Us and Them

The distinction between “us” and “them” is one of the oldest structures of group language.

It is not always wrong. Groups need some sense of internal and external relation. A team must know who is on the team. A community must know whom it serves. A movement must know what it opposes. A family must know who carries familial responsibility.

But “us and them” becomes dangerous when it hardens into absolute moral division.

We are pure; they are corrupt.
We are rational; they are irrational.
We are human; they are less human.
We are victims; they are only oppressors.
We are protectors; they are only threats.
We are truth; they are lies.

Such language reduces complexity and prepares conflict. It allows a group to stop listening, stop self-examining, and stop recognizing shared humanity.

Responsible boundary language should preserve moral judgment without totalization.

It may say:

This action is wrong.
This institution is unjust.
This group holds power in this situation.
This behavior must be resisted.
This harm must be named.

But it should be careful before saying:

They are nothing but evil.
They cannot change.
They deserve no consideration.
Their suffering does not matter.

The first form can support justice. The second form prepares dehumanization.

14.11 Names and Historical Memory

Group names often carry history.

A name may preserve migration, struggle, work, region, ancestry, faith, profession, resistance, trauma, achievement, or founding promise. It can function as compressed memory.

To say the name is to invoke the story.

This is why names can become sacred or contested. They may carry suffering that outsiders do not understand. They may carry pride that members wish to protect. They may carry old wounds, old victories, old humiliations, or old duties.

Changing a name can therefore feel like changing history.

Sometimes name change repairs harm. It removes a degrading label or corrects an inherited injustice. Sometimes name change feels like erasure because the name carried memory that people do not want to lose.

The question is not simply whether a name is old or new. The question is what memory the name preserves and what relation to reality it creates now.

A responsible group must ask:

What history does this name carry?
Who was included in that history?
Who was excluded?
Does the name still fit our reality?
Does it preserve dignity or preserve harm?
Can it be revised without destroying memory?

Names are small forms with long shadows.

14.12 Naming and Collective Voice

A group that can name itself can begin to speak.

Collective voice emerges when members can say not only “I” but “we.” This does not mean every member agrees on everything. It means the group has enough shared identity and concern to produce public language in its own name.

Collective voice may appear in statements, declarations, songs, chants, manifestos, letters, rituals, testimony, demands, or shared stories.

It says:

We are here.
We have experienced this.
We believe this matters.
We ask for recognition.
We reject this treatment.
We claim this responsibility.
We intend to act.

Naming is the precondition of such voice. Without a name, the group struggles to appear as a speaking subject.

But collective voice must be accountable. A few people may speak as “we” while excluding others within the group. Leaders may claim to represent the group while silencing internal difference. Institutions may speak on behalf of people who did not consent.

A collective voice is legitimate when it remains connected to the people in whose name it speaks.

14.13 Internal Difference

No group is completely uniform.

Every group contains internal difference: age, gender, class, region, experience, belief, status, role, memory, personality, interest, and power. A group name can create unity, but it can also hide these differences.

The danger of naming is that the name may become too smooth.

It may imply that all members share the same experience. It may erase minority voices within the group. It may allow dominant members to define the group’s identity for everyone else.

For example, a profession may speak as if all members share one interest, while junior and senior members experience the profession differently. A nation may speak as if all citizens share one memory, while different communities carry different histories. A movement may speak as if all participants share one strategy, while internal debate is real.

Responsible identity language must hold unity and difference together.

It should allow the group to say “we” without pretending that “we” has no internal structure.

A mature group can say:

We share this identity, but we do not all experience it the same way.
We speak together here, but not every voice has equal power inside us.
We need unity, but unity must not erase internal truth.

14.14 Renaming

Renaming is one of the strongest acts of identity transformation.

To rename is to declare that the old language no longer fits.

A group may rename itself to reject stigma, mark a new political consciousness, recover historical memory, clarify mission, escape imposed categories, or signal a change in values.

Renaming says:

We are not what we were called.
We are not what we used to be.
We will no longer live under that frame.
We are choosing a different public meaning.

Renaming can be liberating. It can give a group new dignity, energy, and direction.

But renaming can also be superficial. A group may change its name without changing its structure. An institution may adopt new language while preserving old power. A company may replace harsh labels with softer ones while leaving the underlying practice unchanged.

Renaming must be tested by reality.

Does the new name change recognition?
Does it change behavior?
Does it change rights, responsibilities, or relations?
Does it repair harm?
Does it clarify truth?
Or does it merely improve appearance?

A new name is meaningful when it opens a new structure of relation.

14.15 Misnaming

Misnaming occurs when language gives the wrong identity to a person, group, action, or situation.

Misnaming can happen through ignorance, prejudice, institutional convenience, strategic distortion, or inherited vocabulary. It may be accidental or deliberate.

A protest is misnamed as disorder to avoid its demand.
A structural problem is misnamed as personal failure.
A harmed group is misnamed as overly sensitive.
A controlling relationship is misnamed as care.
A dependent group is misnamed as lazy.
A demand for dignity is misnamed as arrogance.
A critique of authority is misnamed as disloyalty.

Misnaming matters because action follows name.

If the name is wrong, the response will likely be wrong.

To correct misnaming is to reopen reality. It is to say:

This is not what you called it.
The category is false.
The frame is hiding the real structure.
We need a better name before we can act rightly.

Rhetorical clarity often begins by refusing the wrong name.

14.16 Naming and Power

Power often works through naming.

Those with power can define categories, assign labels, write official records, control public vocabulary, and decide which names become legitimate. They can name themselves as protectors, reformers, guardians, experts, or representatives. They can name others as threats, burdens, failures, dependents, extremists, outsiders, or problems.

Such naming can shape the entire field of judgment.

If the powerful can name the situation, they can often control what questions are asked. If they can name the affected people, they can influence whether those people are heard. If they can name the conflict, they can shape what solutions seem reasonable.

Counter-power often begins by renaming.

It says:

This is not security; this is control.
This is not tradition; this is exclusion.
This is not efficiency; this is exploitation.
This is not neutrality; this is silence.
This is not discipline; this is humiliation.
This is not care; this is domination.

Renaming contests the dominant frame.

This is why struggles over words are rarely “just semantics.” Words decide what kind of reality becomes publicly available.

14.17 Naming and Responsibility

A name can assign responsibility.

When a group calls itself “guardians,” “professionals,” “citizens,” “parents,” “teachers,” “builders,” “researchers,” or “leaders,” the name carries expectations. It does not merely identify. It obligates.

To accept the name is to accept a standard.

A teacher must teach.
A doctor must care.
A citizen must participate.
A leader must carry consequence.
A professional must uphold standards.
A parent must protect and guide.
A builder must create something that can stand.

Identity names become empty when they are separated from responsibility.

A group that claims a noble name but refuses its obligation weakens the name itself. A leader who will not carry responsibility turns leadership into status. A profession that will not uphold standards turns professional identity into branding. A community that speaks of care but abandons its vulnerable members turns belonging into rhetoric.

The ethical test of a group name is whether the group lives the responsibility the name implies.

14.18 Naming and Action

Names prepare action.

Once a group has a name, people can organize around it. They can form institutions, write statements, create rituals, gather members, demand recognition, defend rights, preserve memory, and mobilize.

A name allows coordination because it provides a common reference point.

Who is acting?
In whose name?
For whose benefit?
Against what condition?
Under what identity?
With what responsibility?

Action without identity may be scattered. Identity without action may become symbolic. The strongest group names connect recognition to consequence.

They allow members to say:

Because we are this, we must do that.

This is the movement from naming to responsibility, and from responsibility to collective action.

14.19 The Ethics of Naming

Because naming creates identity, boundary, and social reality, it carries ethical responsibility.

A responsible name should clarify rather than distort.
It should recognize dignity rather than produce stigma.
It should create boundary without dehumanization.
It should preserve memory without trapping the group in the past.
It should support unity without erasing internal difference.
It should assign responsibility without creating false guilt.
It should be open to revision when reality changes.

An irresponsible name may reduce, insult, manipulate, erase, exaggerate, or control.

The key ethical questions are:

Who is naming whom?
Does the named group accept the name?
What history does the name carry?
What boundary does it create?
What emotion does it activate?
What action does it prepare?
What responsibility does it assign?
What reality does it make visible or invisible?

Naming is not a minor act. It is the beginning of social architecture.

14.20 Chapter Summary — Naming Ourselves and Others

This chapter explains how language creates group identity, distinction, and social boundary. A group begins to exist publicly when it can be named. Naming turns scattered individuals into a recognizable collective subject. It allows people to say who they are, what they share, what they remember, what they refuse, and what responsibility they claim.

The chapter distinguishes self-naming from other-naming. Self-naming gives a group agency over its own identity, while other-naming may recognize, classify, distort, or dominate. Many identity struggles are therefore struggles over who has the authority to name and what meaning the name carries.

Naming also creates boundaries. The language of “we” and “they” defines belonging, responsibility, difference, and exclusion. Boundaries are necessary for collective life, but they become dangerous when they harden into superiority, hostility, or dehumanization. Responsible naming distinguishes without degrading.

The chapter also examines dignity, stigma, institutional classification, renaming, misnaming, and the power of collective voice. Names can give dignity, preserve memory, and prepare action. But names can also stigmatize, erase complexity, hide power, and force people into false categories.

The central claim is that naming is the first architecture of group identity. To name a group is to create a social object that can be recognized, addressed, defended, contested, and mobilized. Naming does not merely describe groups; it helps form the world in which groups know who they are, who belongs, where boundaries stand, and what action should follow.
