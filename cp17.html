18  Language, Power, and the Organization of Reality

Language does not move through an empty world.

It moves through power.

Some people can speak and be heard. Others speak and are ignored. Some words become official. Others remain informal, suspicious, or invisible. Some definitions become law, policy, standard, curriculum, or public common sense. Others are treated as emotional, extreme, unprofessional, naive, or illegitimate.

Power shapes the conditions under which language operates.

It affects who can name reality, whose account is trusted, which categories become institutional, which stories become public memory, which questions can be asked, which emotions are acceptable, which identities are recognized, and which actions become possible.

Language and power are therefore deeply connected.

Power uses language to organize reality.
Language gives power its public form.
Power defines categories.
Language makes those categories usable.
Power authorizes institutions.
Language makes authorization appear legitimate.
Power controls visibility.
Language determines what can be seen, said, recorded, and acted upon.

This chapter studies how language interacts with power in shaping what can be said, believed, and acted upon.

18.1 Power as the Capacity to Shape Reality

Power is not only the ability to force behavior.

Power is also the ability to shape the field in which people understand reality.

A person with power may not need to command directly. They may shape the categories through which others think. They may define what counts as a problem, what counts as evidence, who counts as credible, which emotions are appropriate, and what solutions are realistic.

This is deeper than coercion.

Coercion says:

Do this, or you will suffer consequences.

Reality-shaping power says:

This is what the situation is.
This is what counts as normal.
This is what can be questioned.
This is what cannot be questioned.
This is who has authority.
This is what kind of action is possible.

When power reaches this level, people may act within its language without feeling directly forced. They may treat inherited categories as natural. They may repeat dominant frames as common sense. They may judge themselves through standards they did not create.

Power becomes strongest when it no longer appears as power.

It appears as reality.

18.2 The Power to Name

One of the first powers is the power to name.

To name something is to make it available for recognition. But naming also controls interpretation. The name tells people what kind of thing they are looking at.

A government may name a protest as disorder.
A company may name layoffs as restructuring.
A family may name control as care.
A school may name humiliation as discipline.
A platform may name dependency as engagement.
An institution may name criticism as disruption.
A profession may name exclusion as standards.

Each name organizes response.

If something is named disorder, control becomes easier to justify.
If it is named restructuring, human loss becomes administrative adjustment.
If it is named care, domination can hide behind affection.
If it is named discipline, harm can appear educational.
If it is named engagement, addiction can appear participation.
If it is named disruption, critique can be contained.
If it is named standards, gatekeeping can appear neutral.

Naming power is the power to establish the first frame.

Those who name first often shape the debate that follows.

18.3 The Power to Define

Definition goes deeper than naming.

Naming says what something is called. Definition says what the name means, what it includes, what it excludes, and what consequence follows from being placed inside it.

Definitions determine recognition.

What counts as harm?
What counts as professionalism?
What counts as competence?
What counts as merit?
What counts as safety?
What counts as loyalty?
What counts as extremism?
What counts as evidence?
What counts as success?

These definitions are never merely semantic when they affect rights, status, opportunity, protection, punishment, or legitimacy.

If “merit” is defined narrowly, inherited advantage may appear as deserved achievement.
If “professionalism” is defined through dominant cultural style, difference may be treated as deficiency.
If “safety” is defined only by those in authority, the fear of the less powerful may disappear.
If “loyalty” is defined as silence, truth-telling becomes betrayal.
If “evidence” is defined too narrowly, lived experience may become inadmissible.

The power to define is the power to decide what reality can count.

A group may suffer, but if its suffering does not fit the accepted definition of harm, the system may refuse to respond.

18.4 Official Language

Power often becomes durable through official language.

Official language appears in laws, policies, contracts, reports, school records, medical records, court judgments, corporate statements, government notices, institutional categories, and administrative forms.

Official language matters because it does not merely express opinion. It enters systems.

A person officially becomes eligible or ineligible.
A case becomes open or closed.
A claim becomes accepted or denied.
A behavior becomes violation or compliance.
A person becomes citizen, employee, patient, student, suspect, applicant, beneficiary, or offender.
A decision becomes authorized.
A record becomes permanent.

Official language creates recognized social facts.

This gives official language enormous power. It can protect people, allocate resources, recognize rights, preserve accountability, and coordinate complex systems. But it can also misclassify, exclude, erase, delay, and dominate.

A reality not recognized in official language may remain socially weak even if it is experientially real.

This is why access to official naming is a form of power.

18.5 What Can Be Said

Power shapes the boundary of the speakable.

In every group, some things can be said easily. Others are difficult, dangerous, shameful, or forbidden. The boundary may be enforced by law, policy, punishment, ridicule, exclusion, professional risk, family shame, social pressure, or internalized fear.

A workplace may make it difficult to say, “The leadership decision created the failure.”

A family may make it difficult to say, “This tradition has harmed us.”

A profession may make it difficult to say, “Our standards are not neutral.”

A state may make it difficult to say, “This authority is illegitimate.”

A community may make it difficult to say, “Our group also caused harm.”

A movement may make it difficult to say, “Our method is producing damage.”

The unsayable is not always explicitly banned. Often, people learn the boundary through consequences.

Who gets punished?
Who gets mocked?
Who is called disloyal?
Who loses opportunity?
Who is labeled difficult?
Who becomes isolated after speaking?

Power does not only silence by censorship. It also silences by making speech costly.

18.6 What Can Be Believed

Power also shapes belief.

It does this by shaping credibility structures. Some speakers are treated as rational, expert, objective, mature, professional, patriotic, respectable, or trustworthy. Others are treated as emotional, biased, immature, dangerous, self-interested, ignorant, or unreliable.

Belief is not distributed equally.

The same sentence can be heard differently depending on who speaks.

A senior leader names risk and is called strategic.
A junior worker names the same risk and is called negative.

A dominant group expresses anger and is called passionate.
A weaker group expresses anger and is called threatening.

An institution speaks abstractly and is called neutral.
An affected person speaks from experience and is called subjective.

A powerful person makes a mistake and is given context.
A less powerful person makes a mistake and is given a label.

Power shapes whose language receives generous interpretation.

This matters because credibility determines whether language can enter public reality. If some speakers are structurally distrusted before they speak, then their knowledge may remain invisible even when accurate.

Power can therefore organize ignorance.

It can make a society or institution unable to believe the people closest to certain realities.

18.7 What Can Be Acted Upon

Language becomes powerful when it creates action pathways.

A reality may be spoken but still not acted upon. For action to occur, the reality must fit available categories, procedures, authorities, resources, and legitimacy structures.

A person may say, “This is unfair,” but if the institution has no category for the unfairness, nothing happens.

A worker may say, “This process is unsafe,” but if the system treats only documented incidents as valid, early warning disappears.

A student may say, “I need support,” but if the school recognizes only failure after grades collapse, prevention becomes impossible.

A citizen may say, “This policy harms us,” but if the decision process has no participation channel, the claim remains outside action.

Power organizes actionability.

It determines which statements become reports, which reports become cases, which cases become decisions, which decisions become changes, and which changes become enforced.

A reality becomes actionable only when language can connect it to institutional response.

This is why naming alone is not enough. The name must enter a structure that can respond.

18.8 Language as Permission

Power often operates by granting or denying permission.

Language tells people what they may do, what they must not do, what they are entitled to claim, what they are allowed to ask, and what they are expected to endure.

Permission language includes:

You may.
You may not.
You are allowed to.
You are not authorized to.
You have the right to.
You are prohibited from.
You are expected to.
You must obtain approval before.
You are not eligible.

These phrases shape action directly.

But permission is not only formal. Groups also create informal permission.

Is it safe to disagree?
Is it acceptable to admit uncertainty?
Is it allowed to ask for help?
Is it possible to challenge a senior person?
Is it acceptable to refuse overwork?
Is it permitted to name harm?
Is it legitimate to leave?

Power lives in the answers to these questions.

When people lack permission language, they may endure conditions they could have challenged. When permission is explicit, action becomes more possible.

Sometimes liberation begins with a sentence:

You are allowed to say no.
You are allowed to ask why.
You are allowed to name what happened.
You are allowed to leave.
You are allowed to expect dignity.

Permission language can reorganize a person’s sense of possible action.

18.9 Language as Constraint

Language also constrains.

A group can be trapped by the terms available to it. If the language of a system offers only certain categories, people may struggle to think or act outside them.

A company that speaks only in revenue terms may struggle to recognize trust until trust becomes measurable loss.

A school that speaks only in grades may struggle to see learning as curiosity, confidence, or development.

A family that speaks only in duty may struggle to recognize emotional need.

A government that speaks only in security terms may struggle to recognize dignity or dissent.

A profession that speaks only in technical terms may struggle to recognize ethical consequence.

The available language creates the available world.

This is not absolute. People can invent new terms, borrow terms, tell stories, and resist existing vocabulary. But language sets the initial boundary of possibility.

To expand language is to expand possible recognition.

To narrow language is to narrow possible action.

18.10 Silence as Power

Silence is not always absence.

Silence can be produced, enforced, rewarded, or strategically maintained. A topic may remain unspoken because speaking would threaten authority, expose contradiction, create obligation, or disturb a favorable image.

Power can create silence by direct prohibition.

Do not discuss this.
Do not record this.
Do not ask that question.
Do not use that word.

But power can also create silence indirectly.

People learn that raising certain issues leads to punishment. They learn that certain pain will be dismissed. They learn that certain questions mark them as difficult. They learn that some realities are too costly to name.

Silence protects power because unnamed realities are harder to organize around.

What cannot be said cannot easily become evidence.
What cannot become evidence cannot easily become complaint.
What cannot become complaint cannot easily become procedure.
What cannot become procedure cannot easily become change.

Breaking silence is therefore a political and rhetorical act.

It turns private knowledge into public possibility.

18.11 Euphemism and Soft Power

Power often uses euphemism.

A euphemism softens, disguises, or sanitizes a reality. Sometimes this is humane. Not every harsh truth needs brutal language. But euphemism becomes dangerous when it hides responsibility, reduces moral weight, or protects the powerful from consequences.

“Collateral damage” can hide civilian death.
“Downsizing” can hide human loss of livelihood.
“Enhanced interrogation” can hide torture.
“Letting people go” can hide firing.
“Performance management” can hide coercive pressure.
“Strategic realignment” can hide failure.
“Behavioral issue” can hide institutional mistreatment.

Euphemism often makes violence, harm, or exploitation administratively tolerable.

It allows people to participate without fully facing what they are participating in.

The ethical question is:

Does this softer language preserve dignity, or does it hide reality?

Responsible language may be gentle, but it should not be evasive.

18.12 Abstraction and Distance

Power also uses abstraction.

Abstract language can be useful. It allows complex systems to generalize, compare, plan, and coordinate. But abstraction can create distance from human consequence.

An institution may speak of “resource allocation” instead of people losing support.
A company may speak of “headcount reduction” instead of workers losing jobs.
A government may speak of “population management” instead of lives being controlled.
A school may speak of “disciplinary outcomes” instead of children being humiliated or excluded.

Abstraction can make action easier by reducing emotional friction.

This may be necessary in some contexts. Decision-makers cannot function if every policy discussion collapses under full emotional weight. But abstraction becomes dangerous when it removes the human meaning of action.

Power often prefers abstraction because abstraction protects decision-makers from the felt reality of consequence.

Responsible institutional language must reconnect abstraction to human impact.

It must be able to say both:

This is the system-level category.

And:

This is what it means for the people affected.

18.13 The Passive Voice of Power

Power often hides itself in grammar.

Passive voice can remove the actor from the sentence.

Mistakes were made.
Concerns were raised.
The policy was implemented.
The request was denied.
The issue was mishandled.
The harm was regrettable.

These sentences describe events without naming agents.

Sometimes this is appropriate. The actor may be unknown, irrelevant, or intentionally backgrounded for good reason. But when responsibility matters, passive language can protect power.

Compare:

“The request was denied.”

With:

“The review committee denied the request because it did not meet the eligibility criteria.”

Compare:

“Mistakes were made.”

With:

“We approved the launch despite unresolved safety warnings.”

The second form is more accountable because it names agency.

Power becomes harder to challenge when no one appears to act. Events seem to happen by themselves. Harm appears as unfortunate occurrence rather than consequence of decisions.

Accountable language restores agency.

It asks: who did what, under what authority, with what consequence?

18.14 The Language of Neutrality

Power often presents itself as neutral.

Neutrality language says:

We are only applying the rules.
We are only following procedure.
We are only using objective criteria.
We are only enforcing standards.
We are not taking sides.
This is just how the system works.

Sometimes neutrality is real and valuable. Systems need procedures, consistency, evidence, and restraint. Neutral language can protect against arbitrary decision.

But neutrality can also hide power.

A rule may appear neutral while producing unequal effects. A standard may appear objective while reflecting the history of one group. A procedure may appear fair while excluding people who cannot navigate it. A platform may claim neutrality while its design amplifies certain voices. A school may apply the same rule to unequal situations and call it fairness.

Neutrality must be tested by consequence.

Who benefits from this neutral language?
Who is burdened by it?
What assumptions are hidden inside the standard?
What history produced the rule?
What alternatives are excluded?
Does neutrality here protect fairness, or does it protect the status quo?

Neutrality is legitimate only when it remains accountable to reality.

18.15 The Language of Naturalization

Power becomes stable when social arrangements appear natural.

Naturalization is the process by which constructed realities are made to seem inevitable, obvious, or beyond question.

People say:

That is just how things are.
This is human nature.
This is how business works.
This is how families work.
This is how the market works.
This is how serious people behave.
This is the real world.

Naturalization removes history and choice.

It makes current arrangements appear as if no one made them and no one can change them. It turns social decisions into natural facts.

This is one of power’s strongest linguistic moves.

If a system is natural, then resistance seems naive.
If a hierarchy is natural, then equality seems unrealistic.
If overwork is natural, then rest seems weakness.
If silence is natural, then truth-telling seems disruption.
If domination is natural, then liberation seems disorder.

Counter-language denaturalizes.

It says:

This was built.
This was chosen.
This is maintained.
This benefits some and burdens others.
This can be changed.

To denaturalize is to restore agency to reality.

18.16 The Language of Legibility

Power needs legibility.

To govern, manage, regulate, or administer, power must make people and situations readable. It creates categories, forms, records, numbers, identities, statuses, and procedures.

Legibility allows systems to coordinate.

It makes taxation possible.
It makes education systems possible.
It makes healthcare systems possible.
It makes legal systems possible.
It makes employment systems possible.
It makes public policy possible.

But legibility can also reduce complexity.

A person becomes a file.
A community becomes a demographic.
A need becomes an eligibility criterion.
A life becomes a data point.
A conflict becomes a case.
A worker becomes a performance score.

The institution sees what its language allows it to see.

This can help or harm. If the category fits, support becomes possible. If the category fails, the person may disappear inside the system.

Power organizes reality by making it legible to itself.

The ethical question is whether institutional legibility remains accountable to human complexity.

18.17 Agenda Setting

Power shapes reality by setting the agenda.

Agenda setting means determining what is discussed, what is delayed, what is ignored, and what receives collective attention.

A leader controls the meeting agenda.
A media system controls public visibility.
A school controls curriculum.
A government controls policy priorities.
A company controls strategic focus.
A family controls which issues are “worth talking about.”

Agenda setting shapes reality because attention is limited.

What appears on the agenda becomes discussable.
What remains off the agenda remains weak.
What is repeatedly delayed becomes practically denied.
What is treated as urgent receives resources.
What is treated as marginal receives little protection.

The power to decide what is worth talking about is the power to decide what can become actionable.

A group may say, “Now is not the time.” Sometimes that is true. But repeated delay can become suppression.

The question is:

Who controls the agenda, and what reality remains outside it?

18.18 Standard Setting

Power also sets standards.

Standards define what counts as good, acceptable, professional, rigorous, safe, qualified, successful, efficient, beautiful, normal, or mature.

Standards can elevate practice. They can protect quality, safety, fairness, and trust.

But standards can also exclude.

If the standard reflects only one group’s history, style, resources, or worldview, it may appear neutral while functioning as a gate. If the standard is vague, powerful people may apply it selectively. If the standard is impossible, it can discipline people into permanent failure.

Language makes standards visible or invisible.

A good standard can be explained:

What is being evaluated?
Why does this criterion matter?
How is it measured?
Who defined it?
Can it be challenged?
Does it predict the quality it claims to measure?
What does it exclude?

A bad standard hides behind authority.

It says:

This is just the standard.

Standard-setting power shapes who advances, who is trusted, who is excluded, and who must adapt.

18.19 The Power to Record

Power records.

What enters the record can become durable. What stays outside the record can disappear.

Records include documents, data, minutes, files, transcripts, archives, reports, grades, diagnoses, performance reviews, criminal records, contracts, policy histories, and public statistics.

Recording gives language persistence.

A statement made in a record may outlive the person who made it. It may follow someone across institutions. It may affect future decisions. It may become evidence. It may become memory.

The power to record is therefore the power to shape future reality.

If the record says “noncompliant,” the future reader sees one reality.
If it says “unable to comply because instructions were inaccessible,” the future reader sees another.

If the record says “employee lacks leadership presence,” one reality appears.
If it says “employee challenged unresolved strategic risks,” another appears.

Records are not neutral memory. They are structured accounts.

Who writes the record?
Who can correct it?
Whose perspective enters?
What language is used?
What categories are available?
What consequences follow?

Control over records is control over institutional memory.

18.20 The Power to Forget

Power does not only record. It also forgets.

Forgetting can be accidental, but it can also be organized.

Records disappear.
Archives remain closed.
Old harms are renamed.
Past decisions lose their rationale.
Uncomfortable histories are excluded from curriculum.
Certain victims are not commemorated.
Certain failures are not documented.
Certain promises are not preserved.

Forgetting protects power by breaking continuity.

If people cannot remember a pattern, each harm appears isolated.
If people cannot access history, authority can claim innocence.
If people forget old promises, institutions avoid accountability.
If past resistance is erased, present resistance feels impossible.

Memory is a resource of accountability.

This is why struggles over archives, records, education, monuments, and public history are struggles over power.

To remember is to make the past available for present judgment.

18.21 The Power to Make Alternatives Unthinkable

Power shapes reality most deeply when it makes alternatives seem impossible.

People may not need to be told “do not change this.” They may simply lack language for another way.

They may say:

There is no alternative.
That would never work.
That is unrealistic.
People are just like that.
The system cannot function otherwise.
You have to accept reality.

Sometimes alternatives truly are limited. But often, what is called impossible is merely outside the dominant frame.

Language can close possibility.

It can make exploitation sound necessary.
It can make hierarchy sound natural.
It can make reform sound naive.
It can make care sound inefficient.
It can make dignity sound impractical.
It can make justice sound disruptive.

Counter-language reopens possibility.

It names alternatives, creates models, tells different stories, and gives people words for futures not yet institutionalized.

A possible future often becomes thinkable before it becomes buildable.

18.22 Counter-Language

Where power organizes reality through language, resistance often begins with counter-language.

Counter-language renames, reframes, redefines, reclassifies, remembers, exposes, and refuses.

It says:

This is not normal.
This is not inevitable.
This is not care.
This is not neutrality.
This is not efficiency.
This is not failure of individuals.
This is a pattern.
This has a name.
This can be changed.

Counter-language is not merely oppositional. At its best, it makes hidden reality visible. It gives people words for experiences that dominant language has misnamed or silenced.

Counter-language can turn private pain into public issue.
It can turn scattered cases into pattern.
It can turn shame into dignity.
It can turn obedience into question.
It can turn silence into testimony.
It can turn isolated individuals into a group.

But counter-language also carries responsibility. It can become simplistic, reactive, or captured by its own slogans. It must remain answerable to evidence and consequence.

The goal is not merely to reverse power’s language. The goal is to build a more truthful language for shared reality.

18.23 Language and Internalized Power

Power does not remain outside the person.

It can become internal.

People may begin to speak to themselves in the language of the systems that govern them. They may judge themselves by standards that were imposed on them. They may call their own needs weakness, their own anger irrational, their own ambition arrogance, their own pain overreaction, their own exhaustion lack of discipline.

Internalized power is power that has become self-language.

The person no longer needs external correction because the corrective voice has moved inside.

This is one of the deepest effects of rhetorical power. It shapes not only public speech, but inner speech.

Liberating language often begins when people notice this internalized vocabulary.

Why do I call this weakness?
Who taught me that asking is shameful?
Why do I call rest laziness?
Why do I treat disagreement as danger?
Why do I believe I must accept this?
Whose standard am I using to judge myself?

Changing language can change the structure of self-recognition.

18.24 Power and Emotional Language

Power regulates emotion.

It tells people which emotions are acceptable, which are excessive, which are professional, which are shameful, which are dangerous, and which are legitimate.

Some people are allowed anger. Others are required to be polite.
Some people are allowed grief. Others must move on.
Some people are allowed fear. Others are told they are dramatic.
Some people are allowed confidence. Others are called arrogant.
Some people are allowed uncertainty. Others are judged incompetent.

Emotion language is power language because emotions affect credibility.

If a group’s anger is called threat, its claim may be dismissed.
If a leader’s anger is called passion, it may increase authority.
If a worker’s fear is called weakness, the risk may be ignored.
If an institution’s caution is called responsibility, delay may be justified.

Power does not only control what can be said. It controls how feeling is interpreted.

A just language must examine emotional double standards.

Who is allowed to feel what, in public, with credibility?

18.25 Power and the Distribution of Complexity

Power affects who receives complexity.

Some people are interpreted with nuance. Others are reduced to labels.

Powerful actors are often given context:

They were under pressure.
The situation was complicated.
Their intentions were good.
They made a difficult decision.
They should not be judged by one mistake.

Less powerful actors may be flattened:

They are lazy.
They are aggressive.
They are ungrateful.
They are irresponsible.
They are dangerous.
They are not a good fit.

The distribution of complexity is a major rhetorical structure of power.

To receive complexity is to be treated as fully human.
To be reduced to a label is to become easier to manage, dismiss, or punish.

Responsible language should distribute complexity more fairly.

This does not mean excusing harm. It means explaining without dehumanizing. It means allowing context without erasing responsibility. It means refusing to give some people full stories and others only categories.

18.26 Power and Credibility Loops

Power creates credibility loops.

Those who are believed gain more power. Those with power are more likely to be believed. Those who are disbelieved struggle to gain power. Those without power are more easily dismissed.

This loop affects public reality.

A group may lack credibility because it lacks official recognition. It may lack official recognition because it lacks credibility. The loop sustains exclusion.

Breaking this loop requires changes in language and structure.

Affected people must be allowed to testify.
Alternative evidence must be recognized.
Institutional categories must be revised.
Gatekeepers must be questioned.
Records must include missing perspectives.
Definitions of expertise must expand where appropriate.

Credibility is not merely personal reputation. It is often structurally distributed.

Power decides who begins with the benefit of the doubt.

18.27 Power as Control of Consequence

Power also determines what happens after speech.

Two people may say the same thing and face different consequences.

One speaks and is promoted.
Another speaks and is punished.

One admits uncertainty and is seen as wise.
Another admits uncertainty and is seen as unqualified.

One breaks a norm and is called innovative.
Another breaks the same norm and is called disrespectful.

One criticizes and is called brave.
Another criticizes and is called difficult.

The consequence of speech shapes future speech.

People learn what is safe. They learn which language leads to opportunity, punishment, recognition, or exclusion. Over time, the group’s speech environment is shaped by these consequences.

Power governs language not only by rules, but by aftermath.

If truth-telling leads to punishment, the group will learn silence.
If dissent leads to exclusion, the group will learn false unity.
If risk reporting leads to blame, the group will learn concealment.
If flattery leads to advancement, the group will learn performance.

The language culture of a group is built by consequences.

18.28 Power and Action Imagination

Power shapes what people can imagine doing.

Language gives names to possible action: refuse, appeal, organize, negotiate, report, resign, repair, resist, comply, disclose, strike, vote, document, escalate, reconcile, redesign, transform.

If people lack action vocabulary, they may feel trapped.

A person may know something is wrong but not know whether it is a complaint, boundary, appeal, negotiation, or exit.

A group may feel frustration but not know whether it is organizing, reform, protest, mutual aid, policy change, or institution-building.

A worker may feel overload but not know whether to call it capacity risk, labor exploitation, prioritization failure, or management issue.

The action name matters because it connects feeling to method.

Power limits action imagination by limiting available language. Counter-power expands action imagination by naming new methods and pathways.

To act, people must first be able to say what kind of action they are taking.

18.29 Language, Power, and Material Reality

Language is powerful, but it is not everything.

Power also operates through money, law, violence, property, technology, geography, infrastructure, labor, bodies, weapons, platforms, and institutions. Language does not replace these forces.

But language organizes how these forces are understood, justified, contested, coordinated, and remembered.

Economic power needs language to call some work skilled and other work unskilled.
Legal power needs language to define rights, violations, and procedures.
Technological power needs language to frame design choices as convenience, innovation, security, or inevitability.
Physical force needs language to justify itself as protection, punishment, order, or defense.
Institutional power needs language to define roles, records, standards, and legitimacy.

Material power becomes socially durable when language makes it meaningful and acceptable.

Therefore, the study of language is not separate from the study of material life. It is one of the ways material power becomes organized inside human reality.

18.30 Responsible Power Language

Power is not inherently evil.

Some power is necessary. Parents need some authority to protect children. Teachers need authority to guide learning. Institutions need power to coordinate complex life. Governments need power to maintain public systems. Organizations need decision rights. Communities need boundaries.

The ethical question is not whether power exists, but how it is linguistically organized and made accountable.

Responsible power language should:

Name agency clearly.
Explain reasons.
Define limits.
Make procedures visible.
Allow questions.
Record accurately.
Preserve rights.
Acknowledge affected people.
Distinguish necessity from choice.
Avoid hiding harm through abstraction.
Make correction possible.

Power becomes more legitimate when its language can be examined.

Irresponsible power language hides agency, naturalizes arrangements, performs neutrality, controls definitions, suppresses alternatives, and makes affected people unreadable.

Responsible power does not fear clear language.

18.31 The Ethics of Counter-Power

Counter-power also needs ethics.

Those who resist dominant language may become careless with their own language. They may simplify enemies, exaggerate innocence, weaponize memory, create new stigmas, or silence internal critique in the name of struggle.

Counter-language can expose domination, but it can also become domination if it refuses accountability.

Ethical counter-power should ask:

Are we naming reality accurately?
Are we giving people complexity?
Are we avoiding dehumanization?
Are we distinguishing responsibility from revenge?
Are we preserving evidence?
Are we allowing internal correction?
Are we building better language, or only inverted language?

The fact that language challenges power does not automatically make it true or just.

All powerful language must answer to reality.

18.32 The Organization of Reality

The central claim of this chapter is that power and language together organize reality.

Power shapes what can be said.
Language gives form to what can be recognized.
Power shapes who can be believed.
Language gives credibility its structure.
Power shapes what can be acted upon.
Language connects meaning to procedure, authority, and action.

Together, language and power define the visible world of a group.

They decide which experiences become public issues, which harms become official categories, which identities receive dignity, which rules appear natural, which institutions appear legitimate, which futures appear possible, and which actions become available.

This is why rhetorical language cannot be studied as style alone.

It is a structure of reality-making under conditions of power.

18.33 Chapter Summary — Language, Power, and the Organization of Reality

This chapter explains how language interacts with power in shaping what can be said, believed, and acted upon. Power is not only the ability to force behavior; it is also the ability to shape the field in which reality is named, defined, interpreted, recorded, and made actionable.

The chapter shows that power operates through naming, definition, official language, agenda setting, standard setting, records, silence, abstraction, neutrality claims, and institutional categories. Those with power often control which realities become visible, which speakers are credible, which harms are recognized, and which actions are permitted.

Language gives power public form. It authorizes roles, legitimizes rules, naturalizes social arrangements, defines standards, and creates official records. But language can also challenge power through counter-naming, reframing, redefinition, testimony, memory, and the creation of new action vocabularies.

The chapter emphasizes that power shapes the boundary of the speakable and believable. Some people are granted complexity, credibility, and official recognition; others are reduced to labels, treated as emotional or unreliable, or excluded from institutional categories. This affects not only public speech, but also inner speech, as people may internalize the language of the systems that govern them.

The central claim is that language and power together organize social reality. They shape what groups can see, what they can believe, what they can question, what they can remember, and what they can do. Responsible rhetorical language must therefore make power visible, name agency clearly, preserve accountability, and keep shared reality open to correction.
