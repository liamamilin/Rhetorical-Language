18  Language, Power, and the Organization of Reality

Language does not move through an empty world; it moves through power. Some people can speak and be heard, while others speak and are ignored. Some words become official while others remain informal, suspicious, or invisible. Some definitions become law, policy, standard, or public common sense, while others are treated as emotional, extreme, or illegitimate. Power shapes the conditions under which language operates: it affects who can name reality, whose account is trusted, which categories become institutional, which stories become public memory, and which actions become possible. Language and power are deeply connected: power uses language to organize reality, and language gives power its public form. This chapter studies how language interacts with power in shaping what can be said, believed, and acted upon.

18.1 Power as the Capacity to Shape Reality

Power is not only the ability to force behavior but also the ability to shape the field in which people understand reality. A person with power may not need to command directly; they may shape the categories through which others think, define what counts as a problem, what counts as evidence, who counts as credible, and what solutions are realistic. This is deeper than coercion. Coercion says do this or suffer consequences; reality-shaping power says this is what the situation is, what counts as normal, what can be questioned, and what cannot. When power reaches this level, people may act within its language without feeling directly forced, treating inherited categories as natural and repeating dominant frames as common sense. Power becomes strongest when it no longer appears as power but appears as reality.

The power to name is one of the first powers. Naming makes something available for recognition, but it also controls interpretation by telling people what kind of thing they are looking at. A government may name a protest as disorder, a company may name layoffs as restructuring, a family may name control as care. Each name organizes response: if something is named disorder, control becomes easier to justify; if named restructuring, human loss becomes administrative adjustment. Naming power is the power to establish the first frame, and those who name first often shape the debate that follows.

The power to define goes deeper than naming. Naming says what something is called; definition says what the name means, what it includes and excludes, and what consequence follows from being placed inside it. Definitions determine recognition: what counts as harm, professionalism, competence, merit, safety, loyalty, extremism, or evidence? These definitions are never merely semantic when they affect rights, status, opportunity, protection, punishment, or legitimacy. If “merit” is defined narrowly, inherited advantage may appear as deserved achievement; if “professionalism” is defined through dominant cultural style, difference may be treated as deficiency. The power to define is the power to decide what reality can count.

Summary of this section. Power shapes the field of understanding before direct command is needed, making inherited categories feel natural. The power to name establishes the first frame that shapes debate. The power to define determines what can be counted as real and what consequences follow from that classification.

18.2 What Can Be Said, Believed, and Acted Upon

Power shapes the boundary of the speakable. In every group, some things can be said easily while others are difficult, dangerous, shameful, or forbidden. The boundary may be enforced by law, punishment, ridicule, exclusion, professional risk, family shame, or internalized fear. A workplace may make it difficult to say “leadership caused the failure”; a family may make it difficult to say “this tradition has harmed us”; a state may make it difficult to say “this authority is illegitimate.” The unsayable is not always explicitly banned; people learn the boundary through consequences: who gets punished, mocked, called disloyal, or isolated after speaking. Power silences not only by censorship but by making speech costly.

Power also shapes belief by shaping credibility structures. Some speakers are treated as rational, expert, and trustworthy while others are treated as emotional, biased, or unreliable. The same sentence can be heard differently depending on who speaks: a senior leader names risk and is called strategic; a junior worker names the same risk and is called negative. A powerful person makes a mistake and is given context; a less powerful person makes a mistake and is given a label. Credibility determines whether language can enter public reality, and if some speakers are structurally distrusted before they speak, their knowledge may remain invisible even when accurate.

Language becomes powerful when it creates action pathways. A reality may be spoken but still not acted upon because it must fit available categories, procedures, authorities, resources, and legitimacy structures. A person may say “this is unfair,” but if the institution has no category for the unfairness, nothing happens. A worker may say “this process is unsafe,” but if the system treats only documented incidents as valid, early warning disappears. Power organizes actionability by determining which statements become reports, which reports become cases, which cases become decisions, and which decisions become changes. A reality becomes actionable only when language can connect it to institutional response.

Summary of this section. Power shapes what can be said by making speech costly, what can be believed by distributing credibility unequally, and what can be acted upon by determining which statements fit institutional response pathways. These three boundaries together define the limits of politically and socially effective speech.

18.3 Language as Permission and Constraint

Power often operates by granting or denying permission. Language tells people what they may do, what they must not do, what they are entitled to claim, and what they are expected to endure. Permission language includes “you may,” “you may not,” “you have the right to,” “you are prohibited from.” But permission is not only formal; groups also create informal permission that answers whether it is safe to disagree, acceptable to admit uncertainty, allowed to ask for help, or possible to challenge a senior person. When people lack permission language, they may endure conditions they could have challenged. Sometimes liberation begins with a sentence: “you are allowed to say no, to ask why, to name what happened, to expect dignity.”

Language also constrains. A group can be trapped by the terms available to it: if a system’s language offers only certain categories, people may struggle to think or act outside them. A company that speaks only in revenue terms may struggle to recognize trust until it becomes measurable loss; a school that speaks only in grades may struggle to see learning as curiosity or confidence; a government that speaks only in security terms may struggle to recognize dignity or dissent. The available language creates the available world, and to expand language is to expand possible recognition.

Official language appears in laws, policies, contracts, reports, court judgments, and administrative forms. It matters because it does not merely express opinion but enters systems where a person officially becomes eligible or ineligible, a case becomes open or closed, a behavior becomes violation or compliance. Official language creates recognized social facts that can protect or exclude, allocate resources or deny them. A reality not recognized in official language may remain socially weak even if experientially real.

Summary of this section. Power grants or denies permission through both formal and informal language, shaping what people feel entitled to do. Language constrains by limiting the categories available for thought and action. Official language creates recognized social facts with material consequences.

18.4 Silence, Euphemism, and Abstraction

Silence is not always absence; it can be produced, enforced, rewarded, or strategically maintained. A topic may remain unspoken because speaking would threaten authority, expose contradiction, create obligation, or disturb a favorable image. Power creates silence through direct prohibition or indirectly through consequences that teach people that raising certain issues leads to punishment. Silence protects power because unnamed realities are harder to organize around: what cannot be said cannot easily become evidence, complaint, procedure, or change. Breaking silence is therefore a political and rhetorical act that turns private knowledge into public possibility.

Euphemism softens or disguises reality. Sometimes this is humane: not every harsh truth needs brutal language. But euphemism becomes dangerous when it hides responsibility or reduces moral weight. “Collateral damage” hides civilian death; “downsizing” hides human loss of livelihood; “enhanced interrogation” hides torture. Euphemism often makes violence or exploitation administratively tolerable by allowing people to participate without fully facing what they are participating in.

Abstraction creates distance from human consequence. An institution may speak of “resource allocation” instead of people losing support, or “headcount reduction” instead of workers losing jobs. Abstraction can make action easier by reducing emotional friction, which is sometimes necessary for decision-makers to function. But abstraction becomes dangerous when it removes the human meaning of action, protecting decision-makers from the felt reality of consequence. Responsible institutional language must reconnect abstraction to human impact.

Power also hides itself in grammar through passive voice that removes the actor from the sentence: “mistakes were made,” “concerns were raised,” “the request was denied.” When responsibility matters, passive language can protect power by describing events without naming agents. Accountable language restores agency by asking who did what, under what authority, with what consequence.

Summary of this section. Silence protects power by preventing realities from being organized and challenged. Euphemism softens harsh realities but can hide responsibility. Abstraction creates distance from human consequence, and passive voice hides agency. All four are linguistic strategies that can protect power from accountability.

18.5 Naturalization, Legibility, and the Power to Forget

Power becomes stable when social arrangements appear natural. Naturalization makes constructed realities seem inevitable, obvious, or beyond question. People say “that is just how things are,” “this is human nature,” “this is how business works.” Naturalization removes history and choice by making current arrangements appear as if no one made them and no one can change them. If a system is natural, resistance seems naive; if a hierarchy is natural, equality seems unrealistic. Counter-language denaturalizes by saying “this was built, this was chosen, this can be changed.”

Power needs legibility. To govern, manage, regulate, or administer, power must make people and situations readable through categories, forms, records, numbers, identities, and procedures. Legibility allows systems to coordinate but can reduce complexity: a person becomes a file, a community becomes a demographic, a life becomes a data point. The institution sees what its language allows it to see, and the ethical question is whether institutional legibility remains accountable to human complexity.

Power also records and forgets. What enters the record becomes durable; what stays outside can disappear. Records include documents, data, minutes, files, archives, reports, and grades. Recording gives language persistence across time, and control over records is control over institutional memory. But power also forgets, either accidentally or through organized disappearance of records, closed archives, renamed harms, or excluded histories. Forgetting protects power by breaking continuity: if people cannot remember a pattern, each harm appears isolated; if past resistance is erased, present resistance feels impossible. Memory is a resource of accountability, and struggles over archives, education, and public history are struggles over power.

Summary of this section. Naturalization makes constructed realities appear inevitable and beyond question; denaturalization restores agency by revealing choice and history. Legibility makes people readable for administration but can reduce complexity. The power to record creates durable memory; the power to forget breaks continuity and protects authority from accountability.

18.6 Chapter Summary

This chapter examines how language interacts with power in shaping what can be said, believed, and acted upon. Power shapes the field of understanding by determining which categories, names, and definitions are available. It is strongest when it no longer appears as power but as reality. Language grants permission or constrains possibility, and silence, euphemism, abstraction, and passive voice can protect power from accountability.

18.6.1 Essential Knowledge

The following points are the core of this chapter and must be understood and retained.

First, power is not only the ability to force behavior but the ability to shape the field in which people understand reality. Power becomes strongest when it no longer appears as power but as reality itself. The most effective power makes its own categories feel natural and inevitable.

Second, the power to name and define is foundational. Whoever establishes the first name or definition shapes the entire subsequent debate. Definitions such as what counts as freedom, safety, fairness, or professionalism determine what can be recognized and what consequences follow. These are never merely semantic.

Third, power shapes the boundaries of the speakable, the believable, and the actionable. Some things cannot be said without cost, some speakers are structurally distrusted, and some realities cannot enter institutional response pathways. These three boundaries define who can effectively speak about what.

Fourth, silence, euphemism, abstraction, and passive voice are linguistic strategies that protect power from accountability. Silence prevents realities from being organized; euphemism hides moral weight; abstraction distances decision-makers from human consequence; passive voice conceals agency. Recognizing these strategies is essential for responsible analysis of institutional language.

Fifth, naturalization makes constructed realities seem inevitable by removing history and choice. Denaturalization restores agency by revealing that current arrangements were built and could be changed. The power to record creates durable memory; the power to forget breaks continuity and protects authority from accountability.
