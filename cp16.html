17  Conflict of Narratives and Competition of Frames

Groups do not only struggle over facts.

They struggle over the meaning of facts.

An event happens, but the event does not enter public life in a neutral form. It is named, framed, narrated, interpreted, justified, blamed, defended, remembered, and connected to identity. Before people decide what should be done, they often fight over what the situation even is.

Is this a crisis or an exaggeration?
Is this reform or destruction?
Is this justice or revenge?
Is this tradition or oppression?
Is this safety or control?
Is this progress or decline?
Is this accountability or attack?
Is this failure or learning?

Each formulation builds a different reality.

This is the central problem of this chapter: groups struggle over shared reality through competing narratives, frames, and definitions.

A frame tells people what kind of situation they are facing.
A narrative tells people what story the event belongs to.
A definition tells people what the central words mean.

When these structures conflict, public disagreement becomes deeper than argument. People are no longer only disagreeing about what to do. They are disagreeing about what world they are in.

17.1 Conflict Beyond Facts

Many conflicts appear to be factual conflicts.

One side presents evidence. The other side presents different evidence. Each side accuses the other of ignoring reality. But underneath the surface, the deeper conflict is often interpretive.

The sides do not only disagree about facts. They disagree about which facts matter, what those facts indicate, what history they belong to, who is responsible, and what kind of response is appropriate.

A company says, “The project failed because the team lacked execution.”

The team says, “The project failed because leadership changed priorities repeatedly.”

Both sides may refer to real facts. But each has a different frame. One frame locates the problem in performance. The other locates it in strategic instability.

A society says, “This protest is disorder.”

The protestors say, “This protest is a response to ignored injustice.”

Both sides may see the same public event. But one frame emphasizes disruption; the other emphasizes cause and grievance.

A family says, “You are being disrespectful.”

The individual says, “I am finally naming what has been hidden.”

Both sides may refer to the same speech act. But one frame treats it as violation of harmony; the other treats it as necessary truth.

In each case, the facts do not resolve the conflict because the facts are being organized by different interpretive architectures.

17.2 Frames Compete Before Arguments Begin

A frame often wins or loses before formal argument begins.

If a group accepts the frame, many conclusions already become easier. If the issue is framed as emergency, urgent action seems natural. If it is framed as moral failure, blame becomes central. If it is framed as technical complexity, expertise becomes central. If it is framed as individual weakness, discipline becomes central. If it is framed as structural misalignment, redesign becomes central.

The frame does not answer every question, but it decides which questions feel relevant.

This is why frame competition is so important.

To win the frame is to shape the field of judgment.

Once a group accepts that a problem is “a communication issue,” it will look for clearer messages, more meetings, better documentation, or improved tone.

Once the same problem is framed as “a power issue,” the group will look at authority, incentives, decision rights, risk distribution, and accountability.

Once it is framed as “a trust issue,” the group will look at credibility, history, consistency, and broken expectations.

Each frame creates a different map.

People may think they are debating solutions, but the deeper battle is over the map on which solutions appear.

17.3 Narrative Conflict

Narrative conflict occurs when groups place the same event inside different stories.

A frame organizes the present. A narrative organizes time.

It tells people:

Where did this begin?
What caused it?
Who acted?
Who suffered?
Who ignored the warning?
Who betrayed whom?
What turning point has arrived?
What future must now be chosen?

The same event can be inserted into multiple narratives.

A reform can be narrated as long-overdue justice or reckless destruction of inherited order.

A technological change can be narrated as liberation, threat, inevitability, exploitation, or progress.

A public scandal can be narrated as one person’s failure, institutional corruption, media exaggeration, political attack, or necessary exposure.

A family conflict can be narrated as rebellion, maturity, betrayal, truth-telling, selfishness, or liberation.

Narratives compete because each story creates different moral meaning.

One story creates heroes.
Another creates villains.
One creates victims.
Another creates troublemakers.
One creates responsibility.
Another creates excuse.
One creates urgency.
Another creates patience.
One creates guilt.
Another creates pride.

To accept a narrative is often to accept a distribution of identity, emotion, and responsibility.

That is why narrative conflict is rarely calm. People are not only defending an interpretation. They are defending the story in which they know who they are.

17.4 Definitions as Battlefields

Definitions are not neutral in public conflict.

The meaning of key words often becomes the site of struggle.

What counts as freedom?
What counts as safety?
What counts as violence?
What counts as fairness?
What counts as respect?
What counts as progress?
What counts as responsibility?
What counts as harm?
What counts as legitimacy?
What counts as truth?

A definition determines what belongs inside a concept and what falls outside it. It decides which cases count, which people are included, which actions are justified, and which demands become intelligible.

For example, if “violence” is defined only as physical injury, then many forms of coercion, threat, humiliation, deprivation, or structural harm may disappear from view.

If “freedom” is defined only as non-interference, then social conditions that make real agency impossible may be ignored.

If “professionalism” is defined as emotional restraint, then justified anger may be treated as incompetence.

If “fairness” is defined as identical treatment, then unequal starting conditions may be ignored.

If “safety” is defined only as protection from external threat, then internal abuse may remain unnamed.

Definitions are therefore instruments of reality construction.

The group that controls definitions often controls what can be recognized.

17.5 Naming the Conflict

Every conflict depends on how the conflict itself is named.

Is this a disagreement, a dispute, a crisis, a misunderstanding, a rebellion, a negotiation, a violation, a correction, a reckoning, a transition, or a war?

The name determines the expected response.

A disagreement invites discussion.
A dispute invites mediation.
A crisis invites emergency action.
A misunderstanding invites clarification.
A rebellion invites suppression or revolution.
A negotiation invites compromise.
A violation invites accountability.
A correction invites adjustment.
A reckoning invites moral review.
A transition invites adaptation.
A war invites mobilization and enemy logic.

This is why naming the conflict is itself part of the conflict.

One side may call it “feedback.” Another may call it “attack.”
One side may call it “boundary-setting.” Another may call it “rejection.”
One side may call it “reform.” Another may call it “destruction.”
One side may call it “truth-telling.” Another may call it “disrespect.”

The conflict cannot be handled well until the parties understand that they are not only fighting over positions. They are fighting over the name of the situation.

17.6 Selection of Evidence

Competing frames select different evidence.

A frame tells people what to look for. Once people stand inside a frame, some evidence becomes obvious while other evidence becomes irrelevant or invisible.

If the frame is “leadership failure,” people look for unclear decisions, inconsistent priorities, poor communication, and lack of accountability.

If the frame is “team underperformance,” people look for missed deadlines, weak ownership, poor execution, and lack of discipline.

If the frame is “systemic constraint,” people look for incentives, workload, resource gaps, process design, and structural contradiction.

Each frame may gather real evidence. But each frame gathers different evidence.

This creates a difficult problem: both sides may appear evidence-based while living inside different evidence-selection systems.

They are not merely arguing over whether facts are true. They are arguing over which facts deserve centrality.

A responsible conflict analysis must therefore ask:

What evidence does each frame make visible?
What evidence does each frame ignore?
What would each side count as proof?
What kind of evidence would threaten the frame?
What evidence is being excluded before discussion begins?

Without these questions, groups may keep exchanging evidence without ever touching the deeper frame conflict.

17.7 The Emotional Force of Frames

Frames carry emotion.

A frame does not merely organize concepts. It tells people what they should feel.

A threat frame produces fear.
A betrayal frame produces anger.
A decline frame produces anxiety or nostalgia.
A progress frame produces hope.
A victim frame produces grievance.
A responsibility frame produces seriousness.
A learning frame produces patience.
A corruption frame produces suspicion.
A dignity frame produces moral resolve.

When groups compete over frames, they also compete over emotional climate.

This is why frame conflict can escalate quickly. If one side frames the situation as danger, and the other frames it as exaggeration, the first side may see the second as irresponsible. If one side frames the issue as injustice, and the other frames it as procedural inconvenience, the first side may see the second as morally blind.

The emotional mismatch becomes part of the conflict.

One group feels fear while another feels irritation.
One group feels humiliation while another feels confusion.
One group feels urgency while another feels caution.
One group feels moral clarity while another feels accused.

If the emotional structure is not recognized, the debate will appear irrational. Each side will think the other is overreacting, underreacting, or acting in bad faith.

17.8 Identity-Protective Narratives

Groups often defend narratives that protect identity.

A narrative does not only explain the world. It protects the group’s sense of itself.

A family may defend the story that “we are loving” even when members are harmed by silence.

A company may defend the story that “we are high-performing” even when the culture burns people out.

A profession may defend the story that “we are objective” even when its standards contain inherited bias.

A nation may defend the story that “we are just” even when history contains injustice.

A movement may defend the story that “we are pure” even when internal power becomes abusive.

Identity-protective narratives are difficult to challenge because the challenge feels existential. It does not merely say, “Your interpretation is incomplete.” It seems to say, “Your group is not who it claims to be.”

This produces resistance.

The group may deny evidence, attack critics, reinterpret harm, or shift blame because accepting the new narrative would require identity revision.

A responsible rhetorical approach must recognize the identity cost of narrative change. It must create language that allows groups to face truth without total collapse.

The goal is not to protect false identity. The goal is to make repair possible.

17.9 Narrative Innocence

Many groups seek narrative innocence.

Narrative innocence is the desire to occupy a story in which one’s own group is purely good, purely harmed, purely rational, purely defensive, or purely misunderstood.

This desire is powerful because innocence removes burden.

If we are only victims, we need not examine our own harm.
If we are only heroes, we need not examine our own aggression.
If we are only rational, we need not examine our own emotion.
If we are only protectors, we need not examine our own control.
If we are only traditional, we need not examine exclusion.
If we are only progressive, we need not examine unintended damage.

Narrative innocence simplifies moral life.

But no human group is purely innocent. Every group has limits, blind spots, contradictions, interests, and internal power relations. A group may be genuinely harmed and still capable of harm. A group may pursue justice and still use unjust methods. A group may preserve real wisdom and still protect harmful traditions.

Mature collective language must move beyond narrative innocence.

It must be able to say:

We were harmed, and we have also harmed.
We were right about this, and wrong about that.
We preserve something valuable, and we must revise something harmful.
We seek justice, and our methods must still be accountable.
We belong together, and we must still listen to internal dissent.

Without this maturity, narrative conflict becomes a struggle between competing forms of innocence.

17.10 Memory Conflict

Groups fight over memory because memory shapes legitimacy.

What happened in the past?
What should be remembered?
What should be forgotten?
Who suffered?
Who sacrificed?
Who betrayed?
Who built?
Who was erased?
What does the past demand from the present?

Memory conflict is not only historical. It is political, moral, institutional, and emotional.

A group that controls memory can shape present authority. It can justify current rules, identity, hierarchy, gratitude, resentment, apology, or repair.

This is why public memory becomes contested.

One group calls an event a founding achievement.
Another calls it an act of exclusion.

One group calls a past system stable order.
Another calls it oppression.

One group calls a leader visionary.
Another calls the same leader harmful.

One group calls an old practice tradition.
Another calls it inherited violence.

Memory conflict is often painful because memory carries identity. To revise memory is to revise the story through which people inherited belonging.

Responsible memory language must be able to hold complexity. It must not erase real achievement, but it must not use achievement to hide harm. It must not preserve injury without allowing renewal. It must not turn memory into mythology.

17.11 The Competition for Common Sense

Frames and narratives compete to become common sense.

Common sense is what a group treats as obvious before argument begins. It is the background language that does not need to defend itself.

When a frame becomes common sense, it becomes extremely powerful.

People no longer say, “This is one interpretation.”
They say, “This is just reality.”

A company may treat constant urgency as common sense.
A family may treat emotional silence as common sense.
A profession may treat its methods as common sense.
A society may treat a social hierarchy as common sense.
A movement may treat its slogans as common sense.
An institution may treat its categories as common sense.

The struggle over common sense is one of the deepest struggles in rhetorical life.

If a group can make its frame feel natural, then opponents must spend energy just to make alternatives visible. They must first denaturalize the dominant frame before they can argue for another one.

Counter-language often begins by saying:

This is not natural.
This is not obvious.
This is not the only way.
This is a frame.
This is a story.
This is a definition that serves someone.

To expose common sense as constructed is to reopen the field of possibility.

17.12 Frame Dominance

Frame dominance occurs when one frame controls the public field so strongly that other frames struggle to appear legitimate.

A dominant frame determines the default vocabulary, the default questions, the default emotions, and the default standards of judgment.

For example, if a public issue is dominated by a security frame, then every proposal must answer security concerns. If it is dominated by an economic frame, then every proposal must justify cost and efficiency. If it is dominated by a moral crisis frame, then compromise may look like cowardice. If it is dominated by a tradition frame, then change must explain itself as continuity or repair.

A dominant frame has agenda-setting power.

It says what the issue is really about.

This does not mean dominant frames are always false. Sometimes a security issue really is central. Sometimes economic cost matters. Sometimes tradition protects real value. Sometimes moral crisis is real.

The problem begins when dominance becomes immunity. A frame becomes dangerous when it cannot be questioned, revised, or supplemented.

No single frame should be allowed to exhaust reality.

17.13 Counter-Framing

Counter-framing challenges a dominant frame.

It does not merely offer a different conclusion. It offers a different way to understand the situation.

Dominant frame: “This is a matter of personal responsibility.”
Counter-frame: “This is a structural incentive problem.”

Dominant frame: “This is disorder.”
Counter-frame: “This is public response to ignored harm.”

Dominant frame: “This is tradition.”
Counter-frame: “This is inherited exclusion.”

Dominant frame: “This is efficiency.”
Counter-frame: “This is cost transferred onto invisible labor.”

Dominant frame: “This is neutrality.”
Counter-frame: “This is silence that protects the status quo.”

Counter-framing is powerful because it changes what becomes discussable.

It may make hidden causes visible.
It may return agency to affected people.
It may expose responsibility.
It may turn private suffering into public issue.
It may create new standards of judgment.

But counter-framing also carries responsibility. It can overcorrect, oversimplify, or create new blind spots. A counter-frame should not become merely an inverted dogma.

The purpose of counter-framing should be fuller reality, not only rhetorical victory.

17.14 Reframing and Repair

Reframing is not always conflictual. Sometimes it is reparative.

A group trapped in destructive conflict may need a new frame that allows movement.

For example:

“This is not a battle over who cares more; it is a conflict over what kind of care is needed.”

“This is not a struggle between tradition and betrayal; it is a question of which parts of tradition still serve life and which parts require repair.”

“This is not proof that the team is incompetent; it is evidence that the system has exceeded its coordination capacity.”

“This is not an attack on the institution; it is an attempt to make the institution worthy of its stated purpose.”

Reframing can reduce defensive conflict by giving people a way to face reality without total humiliation.

A good reparative frame preserves enough truth to be honest and enough dignity to make action possible.

It does not say, “No one is responsible.”
It says, “Responsibility can be faced in a structure that allows repair.”

This kind of reframing is one of the most important rhetorical tools for moving groups out of narrative deadlock.

17.15 Polarization and Frame Lock

Polarization occurs when groups become locked into opposing frames that mutually reinforce each other.

Each side interprets the other side’s language as proof of its own frame.

If one group frames the other as dangerous, every strong statement by the other side confirms danger.

If the other group frames the first as oppressive, every act of control confirms oppression.

Each side’s defensive action becomes evidence for the other side’s narrative.

This creates frame lock.

The conflict becomes self-reinforcing. New evidence does not open the system; it is absorbed into existing frames. Ambiguity disappears. Nuance becomes betrayal. Internal dissent becomes suspect. The other side becomes unreadable except as enemy.

In frame lock, communication continues but understanding collapses.

People speak more, but hear less.
Evidence increases, but interpretation hardens.
Moral language intensifies, but moral imagination shrinks.

Breaking frame lock requires more than better facts. It requires a shift in the structure of interpretation.

The parties must become able to see the frame as a frame.

17.16 Definition Capture

Definition capture occurs when one group successfully fixes the meaning of a key term in a way that serves its interests.

If a company defines “flexibility” as employee availability at all times, then exploitation can appear as adaptability.

If a government defines “security” in a way that includes all dissent, then opposition can appear as threat.

If a family defines “respect” as obedience, then disagreement can appear immoral.

If a profession defines “merit” in a way that ignores unequal access, then inherited advantage can appear deserved.

If a movement defines “loyalty” as agreement, then internal critique can appear betrayal.

Definition capture is powerful because it controls the moral vocabulary.

People may still debate, but the debate happens inside captured terms.

To resist definition capture, language must reopen the concept.

What does this word mean here?
Who defined it?
What cases does it include?
What does it exclude?
What interests does this definition serve?
What alternative definition would reveal hidden reality?

A free public language requires contestable definitions.

17.17 The Role of Institutions in Frame Competition

Institutions are not neutral spectators in frame conflict.

They often stabilize one frame through official language.

A court frames an event legally.
A hospital frames it medically.
A school frames it educationally.
A company frames it operationally.
A government frames it administratively or politically.
A media organization frames it publicly.
A profession frames it technically.

Institutional framing has authority because it becomes official. It enters documents, records, procedures, categories, reports, policies, and decisions.

This official framing can clarify reality. It can also narrow it.

A legal frame may miss emotional truth.
A medical frame may miss social cause.
An operational frame may miss moral harm.
A bureaucratic frame may miss lived experience.
A technical frame may miss political consequence.
A political frame may distort technical evidence.

Institutions must therefore recognize the limits of their frames.

Official language should not pretend that institutional framing is the whole reality.

17.18 Media and Narrative Competition

Media systems amplify narrative competition.

They choose titles, images, sequences, sources, comparisons, and repeated terms. They decide whether an event appears as crisis, scandal, tragedy, conflict, trend, threat, breakthrough, failure, or human-interest story.

At scale, these choices shape public reality.

A headline can frame before the article explains.
An image can create emotion before evidence appears.
A repeated phrase can make one interpretation feel familiar.
A selected quote can define the moral center.
A missing context can distort the whole event.

Media narrative is powerful because most publics encounter events indirectly. They do not see the whole event; they see mediated forms of the event.

This does not mean media frames are always manipulative. Framing is unavoidable. But media language must be accountable because it has disproportionate power in organizing public perception.

The question is not whether media frames. It must frame.

The question is whether the framing helps the public meet reality or traps it inside spectacle, panic, simplification, or tribal confirmation.

17.19 The Internet and Frame Acceleration

Networked communication accelerates frame competition.

A frame can spread before verification. A slogan can harden before complexity appears. A definition can become a badge of identity. A narrative can become viral because it resonates emotionally, not because it is accurate.

In networked publics, speed changes the structure of conflict.

Interpretations travel faster than institutions can respond.
Outrage travels faster than evidence.
Identity markers travel faster than explanation.
Simplified frames travel faster than complex analysis.
Screenshots, clips, hashtags, and slogans become compressed reality.

This creates a public environment where frame competition is continuous and unstable.

A responsible public must therefore develop practices of rhetorical delay.

Before accepting the first frame, ask: what is being selected?
Before repeating the slogan, ask: what is being compressed?
Before joining the narrative, ask: what role am I being invited to play?
Before accepting the definition, ask: what reality does this word make visible or invisible?

Slow interpretation becomes a civic discipline.

17.20 Translation Between Frames

Some conflicts require translation between frames.

Translation does not mean surrendering one’s position. It means making one frame intelligible to people who inhabit another.

A technical group may need to translate risk into business consequence.
A community may need to translate lived harm into institutional categories.
A leader may need to translate strategy into personal meaning.
A reformer may need to translate moral urgency into procedural change.
A critic may need to translate anger into claims that others can examine.

Translation is difficult because frames carry different assumptions, emotions, and standards of proof.

One side asks for data.
Another asks for testimony.
One side asks for procedure.
Another asks for recognition.
One side asks for efficiency.
Another asks for dignity.
One side asks for stability.
Another asks for repair.

A translator must understand both structures.

The goal is not to erase difference, but to create enough shared language for conflict to become discussable.

Without translation, groups may shout from separate worlds.

17.21 Adjudicating Competing Frames

Not all frames are equally valid.

A mature rhetorical theory cannot say, “Everything is just framing.” That would collapse into relativism. Frames must be judged.

A frame should be evaluated by its relation to reality.

Does it explain the evidence?
Does it account for complexity?
Does it identify responsibility accurately?
Does it include affected voices?
Does it clarify stakes?
Does it avoid unnecessary dehumanization?
Does it remain open to correction?
Does it guide action responsibly?
Does it reveal more than it hides?

A good frame does not need to explain everything perfectly. But it should make reality more intelligible and action more responsible.

A bad frame may be emotionally satisfying but reality-distorting. It may explain too easily, blame too quickly, erase too much, or make harmful action feel justified.

Frame judgment is therefore essential.

The point is not to escape framing. The point is to choose and revise frames under the discipline of truth, evidence, dignity, and consequence.

17.22 The Ethics of Narrative Conflict

Narrative conflict must be ethically governed because stories can mobilize enormous emotional force.

A group should not create a story that knowingly hides evidence.
It should not turn opponents into monsters for convenience.
It should not erase victims because they complicate the group’s identity.
It should not claim innocence when responsibility exists.
It should not make trauma into a permanent weapon.
It should not use memory to justify future cruelty.
It should not use progress to excuse present harm.
It should not use tradition to silence repair.

Responsible narrative conflict requires moral discipline.

It can say: our story matters, but it is not beyond correction.
It can say: our pain is real, but it does not make us incapable of harm.
It can say: our opponent is wrong, but not less than human.
It can say: our memory is sacred, but not immune from truth.
It can say: our identity is important, but reality is more important than self-image.

Without this discipline, narrative conflict becomes a struggle for total moral control.

17.23 Productive Conflict

Not all frame and narrative conflict is destructive.

Some conflict is necessary.

A dominant frame may hide real harm. A public narrative may erase victims. An institution may misname people. A group may protect itself with false innocence. In these cases, counter-framing and narrative challenge are essential.

Productive conflict reveals hidden reality.

It makes unspoken experience visible.
It challenges false normality.
It exposes captured definitions.
It repairs memory.
It redistributes responsibility.
It opens new action.

But productive conflict must remain oriented toward reality, not merely victory.

It should increase the group’s ability to see.
It should not only reverse domination.
It should not merely replace one closed story with another.
It should not use truth as a weapon without responsibility.

The best rhetorical conflict does not only defeat a frame. It improves the shared world’s capacity to meet reality.

17.24 The Aim: Shared Reality Without Forced Uniformity

The goal is not to make everyone share one story.

Human life is too complex for a single total narrative. Different groups will remember differently, value differently, and interpret differently. A healthy society or institution must allow plural frames and multiple narratives.

But pluralism is not the same as fragmentation.

Groups need enough shared reality to coordinate, deliberate, and hold one another accountable. They need some common evidence, some shared definitions, some procedures for disagreement, and some boundaries against dehumanization.

The aim is shared reality without forced uniformity.

This means:

Different frames can be heard, but they must be answerable to evidence.
Different narratives can coexist, but they must not erase harm.
Different definitions can be debated, but they must be explicit.
Different identities can speak, but they must not destroy the humanity of others.
Different memories can be preserved, but they must remain open to correction.

This is difficult. But it is the condition of mature public life.

17.25 Chapter Summary — Conflict of Narratives and Competition of Frames

This chapter explains how groups struggle over reality through competing narratives, frames, and definitions. Public conflict is rarely only a disagreement over facts. More often, groups disagree about what kind of situation they are in, which facts matter, what story the event belongs to, who is responsible, and what response is appropriate.

The chapter shows that frames organize the present by defining the issue, selecting evidence, assigning responsibility, shaping emotion, and narrowing possible action. Narratives organize time by placing events inside stories of progress, decline, betrayal, justice, reform, trauma, or renewal. Definitions organize meaning by determining what central words such as freedom, safety, fairness, harm, responsibility, or legitimacy include and exclude.

Frame and narrative conflict are powerful because they are tied to identity. Groups often defend stories that protect their sense of innocence, dignity, history, and belonging. Challenging a narrative may feel like challenging the group’s existence. This is why public conflict often escalates beyond evidence into memory, emotion, and identity.

The chapter also examines frame dominance, counter-framing, definition capture, institutional framing, media amplification, networked acceleration, and frame lock. It argues that not all frames are equally valid. Frames and narratives must be judged by whether they clarify evidence, include affected voices, assign responsibility accurately, avoid dehumanization, remain open to correction, and guide action responsibly.

The central claim is that conflict over language is conflict over shared reality. Groups struggle over the frames, stories, and definitions through which reality becomes publicly visible and actionable. The aim is not forced uniformity, but a shared reality strong enough to support disagreement, accountability, and coordinated life.
