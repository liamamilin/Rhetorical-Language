17  Conflict of Narratives and Competition of Frames

Groups do not only struggle over facts; they struggle over the meaning of facts. An event happens, but it does not enter public life in a neutral form. It is named, framed, narrated, interpreted, justified, blamed, defended, remembered, and connected to identity. Before people decide what should be done, they often fight over what the situation even is. Is this a crisis or an exaggeration, reform or destruction, justice or revenge, tradition or oppression, safety or control, progress or decline? Each formulation builds a different reality. This chapter examines the central problem of groups struggling over shared reality through competing narratives, frames, and definitions.

17.1 Conflict Beyond Facts

Many conflicts appear to be factual conflicts, but underneath the surface the deeper conflict is often interpretive. The sides do not only disagree about facts but about which facts matter, what those facts indicate, what history they belong to, who is responsible, and what kind of response is appropriate. A company says “the project failed because the team lacked execution”; the team says “the project failed because leadership changed priorities repeatedly.” Both sides may refer to real facts, but each has a different frame that locates the problem differently. A society says “this protest is disorder”; the protestors say “this protest is a response to ignored injustice.” In each case, the facts do not resolve the conflict because the facts are being organized by different interpretive architectures.

A frame often wins or loses before formal argument begins. If a group accepts the frame, many conclusions already become easier. If the issue is framed as emergency, urgent action seems natural; if framed as moral failure, blame becomes central; if framed as technical complexity, expertise becomes central. The frame does not answer every question, but it decides which questions feel relevant. This is why frame competition is so important: to win the frame is to shape the field of judgment before argument begins. People may think they are debating solutions, but the deeper battle is over the map on which solutions appear.

Narrative conflict occurs when groups place the same event inside different stories. A reform can be narrated as long-overdue justice or reckless destruction of inherited order; a technological change can be narrated as liberation, threat, inevitability, or exploitation. Narratives compete because each story creates different moral meaning: one creates heroes, another creates villains; one creates responsibility, another creates excuse. To accept a narrative is often to accept a distribution of identity, emotion, and responsibility.

Summary of this section. Public conflicts are often interpretive rather than factual: groups disagree about which facts matter and what they indicate. Frames shape the field of judgment before argument begins, and narratives assign different moral roles and responsibility. The deeper battle is over the interpretive architecture through which facts are understood.

17.2 Frames, Narratives, and Definitions as Battlefields

Definitions are not neutral in public conflict. The meaning of key words becomes a site of struggle: what counts as freedom, safety, violence, fairness, respect, progress, responsibility, or harm? A definition determines what belongs inside a concept and what falls outside it, what cases count, which people are included, which actions are justified, and which demands become intelligible. If “violence” is defined only as physical injury, then many forms of coercion or structural harm may disappear from view. If “fairness” is defined only as identical treatment, then unequal starting conditions may be ignored. Definitions are instruments of reality construction: the group that controls definitions often controls what can be recognized.

Every conflict depends on how the conflict itself is named. Is this a disagreement, dispute, crisis, misunderstanding, rebellion, negotiation, violation, correction, or war? The name determines the expected response: a disagreement invites discussion, a crisis invites emergency action, a rebellion invites suppression or revolution. Naming the conflict is itself part of the conflict: one side calls it feedback while another calls it attack; one calls it reform while another calls it destruction.

Competing frames select different evidence. A frame tells people what to look for, and once people stand inside a frame, some evidence becomes obvious while other evidence becomes irrelevant. If the frame is “leadership failure,” people look for unclear decisions and inconsistent priorities; if the frame is “systemic constraint,” people look for incentives, workload, and structural contradictions. Both sides may appear evidence-based while living inside different evidence-selection systems. Responsible conflict analysis must therefore ask what evidence each frame makes visible and ignores, and what kind of evidence would threaten the frame.

Summary of this section. Definitions determine what can be recognized and counted as real; controlling definitions is a form of power. Naming the conflict shapes expected responses. Competing frames select different evidence, making factual resolution difficult when the disagreement is about which facts matter.

17.3 The Emotional Force of Frames and Identity-Protective Narratives

Frames carry emotion. A threat frame produces fear, a betrayal frame produces anger, a decline frame produces anxiety, a progress frame produces hope, a dignity frame produces moral resolve. When groups compete over frames, they also compete over emotional climate. This is why frame conflict can escalate quickly: if one side frames the situation as danger and the other as exaggeration, the first may see the second as irresponsible; if one frames it as injustice and the other as procedural inconvenience, the first may see the second as morally blind. The emotional mismatch becomes part of the conflict.

Groups often defend narratives that protect identity. A family may defend the story that “we are loving” even when members are harmed by silence; a company may defend the story that “we are high-performing” even when culture burns people out; a nation may defend the story that “we are just” even when history contains injustice. Identity-protective narratives are difficult to challenge because the challenge feels existential: it does not merely say “your interpretation is incomplete” but seems to say “your group is not who it claims to be.” This produces resistance and denial.

Many groups seek narrative innocence: the desire to occupy a story in which one’s own group is purely good, purely harmed, purely rational, purely defensive. If the group is only victims, it need not examine its own harm; if only heroes, it need not examine its own aggression. But no human group is purely innocent. Mature collective language must move beyond narrative innocence to say “we were harmed and we have also harmed,” “we were right about this and wrong about that.” Without this maturity, narrative conflict becomes a struggle between competing forms of innocence.

Summary of this section. Frames carry emotional force that can escalate conflict through emotional mismatch. Identity-protective narratives defend the group’s self-image and resist challenge by treating critique as existential threat. Narrative innocence prevents groups from examining their own complexity and must be overcome for mature collective language.

17.4 Frame Dominance and Counter-Framing

Frame dominance occurs when one frame controls the public field so strongly that other frames struggle to appear legitimate. A dominant frame determines the default vocabulary, questions, emotions, and standards of judgment. If a public issue is dominated by a security frame, every proposal must answer security concerns; if dominated by an economic frame, every proposal must justify cost and efficiency. A dominant frame has agenda-setting power that says what the issue is really about. The problem begins when dominance becomes immunity: a frame becomes dangerous when it cannot be questioned, revised, or supplemented.

Counter-framing challenges a dominant frame by offering a different way to understand the situation. Where the dominant frame says “this is a matter of personal responsibility,” the counter-frame says “this is a structural incentive problem.” Where the dominant frame says “this is disorder,” the counter-frame says “this is public response to ignored harm.” Counter-framing is powerful because it changes what becomes discussable, making hidden causes visible and returning agency to affected people. But counter-framing also carries responsibility: it can overcorrect, oversimplify, or create new blind spots, and its purpose should be fuller reality rather than rhetorical victory.

Reframing is not always conflictual; sometimes it is reparative. A group trapped in destructive conflict may need a new frame that allows movement. “This is not a battle over who cares more but a conflict over what kind of care is needed.” Reframing can reduce defensive conflict by giving people a way to face reality without total humiliation. A good reparative frame preserves enough truth to be honest and enough dignity to make action possible.

Summary of this section. Frame dominance sets the agenda and default terms of debate; it becomes dangerous when immune to questioning. Counter-framing challenges dominant frames by offering alternative interpretations, but must aim at fuller reality rather than mere victory. Reparative reframing can reduce conflict by enabling movement without humiliation.

17.5 Polarization and Frame Lock

Polarization occurs when groups become locked into opposing frames that mutually reinforce each other. Each side interprets the other’s language as proof of its own frame. If one group frames the other as dangerous, every strong statement by the other side confirms danger; if the other frames the first as oppressive, every act of control confirms oppression. This creates frame lock where the conflict becomes self-reinforcing: new evidence does not open the system but is absorbed into existing frames, nuance becomes betrayal, and internal dissent becomes suspect. In frame lock, communication continues but understanding collapses, and people speak more but hear less.

Breaking frame lock requires more than better facts; it requires a shift in the structure of interpretation. The parties must become able to see the frame as a frame. Definition capture occurs when one group successfully fixes the meaning of a key term in a way that serves its interests: a company defines “flexibility” as employee availability at all times so that exploitation appears as adaptability; a government defines “security” to include all dissent so that opposition appears as threat. Definition capture controls moral vocabulary and forces debate to happen inside captured terms.

Media systems amplify narrative competition by choosing titles, images, sequences, and sources that shape public reality. A headline can frame before the article explains, and a repeated phrase can make one interpretation feel familiar. Networked communication accelerates frame competition: frames spread before verification, slogans harden before complexity appears, and simplified frames travel faster than complex analysis. This creates a public environment where frame competition is continuous and unstable, requiring practices of rhetorical delay: before accepting the first frame, ask what is being selected; before repeating the slogan, ask what is being compressed.

Summary of this section. Polarization creates frame lock where opposing frames reinforce each other and block learning. Definition capture controls moral vocabulary and limits debate. Media and networked communication accelerate frame competition, requiring disciplined practices of questioning first frames and slogans.

17.6 Chapter Summary

This chapter examines how groups struggle over shared reality through competing narratives, frames, and definitions. Public conflicts are often interpretive rather than factual: groups disagree not only about facts but about which facts matter and what they mean. Definitions determine what can be recognized as real; controlling definitions is a form of power. Frame dominance, polarization, and definition capture can block collective understanding.

17.6.1 Essential Knowledge

The following points are the core of this chapter and must be understood and retained.

First, many public conflicts are interpretive rather than factual. Groups disagree not only about what happened but about which facts matter, what they indicate, who is responsible, and what kind of situation they are in. Facts alone cannot resolve such conflicts because the disagreement is about the architecture of interpretation itself.

Second, definitions are instruments of reality construction. The meaning of key words such as freedom, safety, violence, fairness, or professionalism determines what can be recognized and counted as real. The group that controls definitions controls what can be seen.

Third, identity-protective narratives defend the group’s self-image and resist evidence that threatens it. A group invested in a story of its own innocence cannot easily acknowledge harm it has caused. Breaking through identity-protective narratives requires separating necessary truth from unnecessary humiliation.

Fourth, frame lock occurs when opposing frames mutually reinforce each other, making each side’s defensive action confirm the other side’s narrative. Breaking frame lock requires not better facts but a shift in the structure of interpretation: the parties must become able to see their own frame as a frame.

Fifth, responsible public language requires awareness of frames, the ability to see one’s own frame as a frame, and the discipline to question inherited interpretations. Before accepting the first frame, ask what is being selected; before repeating the slogan, ask what is being compressed.
